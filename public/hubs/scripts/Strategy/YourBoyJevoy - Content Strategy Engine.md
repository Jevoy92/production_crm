# YourBoyJevoy — Content Strategy Engine
## A Shy Creator's Roadmap to Building an Audience Through Honest Investigation

---

## PART I: THE PROBLEM WITH STARTING SHY

Here's the honest tension. You're shy. You want to build a public-facing creative brand. And you've already figured out one solution — Palmer House gives you a thesis to hide behind. The investigation carries the video. The camera psychology carries the conversation. You don't have to perform as Jevoy; you perform as the investigator.

YourBoyJevoy doesn't have that cover. YourBoyJevoy is supposed to be the unfiltered, creative, personal side. Which means the thing you're most afraid of — being seen as yourself — is the entire point of the channel.

So the question isn't "how do I get comfortable on camera." The question is: **what is the thesis that makes YourBoyJevoy worth watching even before you're comfortable?**

Because here's the thing about shy creators who build real audiences: they almost never start by being comfortable. They start by being *curious*. They find a question they're genuinely obsessed with, and the obsession overrides the self-consciousness. The camera becomes a tool for the investigation, not a spotlight on the person.

Palmer House investigates the psychology of being seen on camera. That's the thesis.

YourBoyJevoy needs its own thesis. Something equally specific, equally investigative, equally grounded in real questions — but personal. Human. Cultural. Honest.

---

## PART II: THE YOURBOYJEVOY THESIS

After reading your existing scripts — sheltered kids, walking on eggshells, hard conversations as love, confidence vs. wisdom, OnlyFans and the Christian whisper — a clear thesis emerges:

> **YourBoyJevoy investigates the gap between what people say and what people actually do — especially in faith, family, and culture.**

That's the thread running through every one of those scripts. Sheltered kids don't become pure kids — they become sneaky kids. That's the gap between the intention (protection) and the outcome (secrecy). Hard conversations are love — that's the gap between what feels loving (avoiding conflict) and what actually is loving (honest engagement). Confidence isn't wisdom — that's the gap between how confidence looks and what it actually produces.

The gap. That's the investigation.

And it's personal because you live in it. You're a Jamaican-born, Seattle-based, Christian, married father of three who is also a dancer, filmmaker, musician, and entrepreneur. You exist at the intersection of multiple cultures, multiple identities, multiple expectations. The gap between what those worlds say and what they actually do is something you've been navigating your entire life.

That's not a weakness. That's the content.

**The full thesis statement:**

> *YourBoyJevoy is a creative investigation into the gap between what people say and what people actually do — in faith, family, culture, and identity. Hosted by someone who lives at the intersection of all four.*

## PART III: THE INVESTIGATIVE LENS FOR YOURBOYJEVOY

Palmer House's lens: **What happens when people are seen, recorded, and judged?**

YourBoyJevoy's lens: **What happens when people say one thing and do another — and why do we keep pretending that's not happening?**

This lens works because:

1. **It's universal.** Everyone has experienced the gap between stated values and actual behavior — in themselves, in their families, in their churches, in their culture.

2. **It's personal without being confessional.** You don't have to expose yourself to explore this. You can explore it through observation, through cultural patterns, through the things everyone notices but nobody says out loud.

3. **It's faith-integrated naturally.** The gap between what Christians say and what Christians do is one of the most documented, most felt, most rarely-addressed topics in faith content. You're not attacking faith — you're investigating the gap between faith as stated and faith as lived. That's actually a deeply theological project.

4. **It's culturally specific without being exclusionary.** The Jamaican-American experience, the Black Christian experience, the immigrant family experience — these are specific enough to be authentic and universal enough to resonate beyond the community.

5. **It's shy-creator friendly.** You don't have to perform. You just have to notice things and say them out loud. The observation is the content. The honesty is the performance.

## PART IV: THE THREE-LANE ARCHITECTURE

Each lane has a distinct identity, audience, and content mode. They cross-pollinate but never collapse into each other.

| Lane | Thesis | Audience | Content Mode | Tone |
|------|--------|----------|--------------|------|
| **Jevoy Palmer** | "Here's how I think about building things" | Entrepreneurs, systems thinkers, founders | Build-in-public, frameworks, strategic reflection | Professional-warm, structured |
| **YourBoyJevoy** | "Here's the gap between what people say and what they do" | Creative souls, faith-curious, culturally honest people | Personal investigation, cultural commentary, honest observation | Casual, playful, Jamaican flavor, raw |
| **Palmer House Productions** | "Here's the psychology of being seen on camera" | Business owners who want to use video | Investigative journalism, long-form documentary | Authoritative, curious, grounded in research |

**How they cross-pollinate without collapsing:**

- A YourBoyJevoy short about "sheltered kids get sneaky kids" can reference Jevoy Palmer's thinking about systems without becoming a business strategy post.
- A Palmer House investigation into why business owners avoid visibility can reference the YourBoyJevoy insight about the gap between stated values and actual behavior without becoming a personal brand post.
- Jevoy Palmer's build-in-public content can reference both — "here's how I'm thinking about the YourBoyJevoy thesis" — because the Jevoy Palmer brand is explicitly about how his mind works.

The rule: **each piece of content has one home.** It can reference the other lanes, but it lives in one lane. The audience for each lane is different enough that cross-contamination is more confusing than helpful.

## PART V: THE SHY CREATOR RAMP-UP PLAN

This is the most important section. Because the strategy is only useful if you can actually execute it while being shy. Here's the progression:

### Stage 1: The "Something on My Mind" Shorts (Months 1-3)

**Format:** 60-90 seconds. One observation. No polish required. Filmed anywhere — kitchen, car, porch, walking.

**Why this works for shy creators:** The format is short enough that the self-consciousness doesn't have time to fully kick in. You say one thing, you're done. The brevity is the feature, not a limitation. And because the format is explicitly "unpolished," there's no standard to fall short of. The rawness IS the standard.

**The opener that makes it work:** "I'm not even gonna lie — something on my mind is…" This opener does three things. It signals honesty (not performance). It signals brevity (one thing). And it gives you permission to be uncertain, unfinished, and real.

**Cadence:** 2-3 times per week. Not daily — that's a recipe for burnout when you're shy and still getting comfortable. 2-3 times per week is enough to build a library without overwhelming yourself.

**Topics:** The gap. Always the gap. Between what people say and what they do. Between the stated value and the actual behavior. Between the cultural expectation and the lived reality.

### Stage 2: The "I've Been Thinking About This" Medium-Form (Months 4-6)

**Format:** 3-5 minutes. Still personal, still observational, but with more development. One observation, explored from multiple angles.

**Why this works:** By month 4, you've recorded 30-40 shorts. The camera is less foreign. The self-consciousness has reduced — not eliminated, but reduced. You're ready to stay in front of the camera a little longer.

**The structure:** Open with the observation (the gap). Explore it from your personal experience. Explore it from cultural observation. Land on an insight. Faith arrives last, quietly.

**Cadence:** Once per week. The shorts continue alongside this.

### Stage 3: The Long-Form Investigation (Months 7+)

**Format:** 10-15 minutes. Full investigative treatment. The gap explored through research, personal experience, cultural history, and psychological insight.

**Why this works:** By month 7, you have a body of work. You know which observations resonate most. You know which gaps your audience cares about most. The long-form videos are the natural extension of the shorts — they're the same observations, gone deeper.

**The structure:** Same as Palmer House — Tension → Exploration → Investigation → Insight → Application. But the application is personal, not business-oriented. "Here's what I'm doing differently because of this" not "here's how to hire someone to help you."

**Cadence:** Once every 2-3 weeks. Quality over quantity at this stage.

## PART VI: THE "SOMETHING ON MY MIND" FORMAT SPEC

This is the operational guide for the shorts series. Print this. Put it next to wherever you film.

**The format:**
- **Length:** 60-90 seconds. Hard cap at 2 minutes.
- **Structure:** One observation. One gap. One honest landing.
- **Opener:** "I'm not even gonna lie — something on my mind is…" OR "Something on my mind is…" (the opener is the brand)
- **Closer:** No formal close needed. End when the thought is complete. "That's it. That's the thought." is a perfectly acceptable ending.
- **Polish level:** Zero required. Real environment. Real lighting. Real you.
- **Filming location:** Wherever you are. Kitchen. Car. Porch. Walking. The location is part of the authenticity.

**The topic formula:**
Every "Something on My Mind" short follows this pattern:
1. Name the gap (what people say vs. what they do)
2. Give one specific example that makes it concrete
3. Land on why it matters

That's it. Three moves. 60-90 seconds.

**The rule that saves you from overthinking:**
You're not making a masterpiece. You're making a practice. A practice can be messy. It's allowed. It's almost better messy — because it sounds human.

**When you can't find a topic:**
Start with: "I'm overwhelmed… and here's what that makes me do."
Then name ONE behavior that reveals a gap.
- "When I'm overwhelmed I get preachy."
- "When I'm overwhelmed I avoid people."
- "When I'm overwhelmed I scroll and call it rest."
Boom. Topic found.

## PART VII: THE LONG-FORM THESIS TOPICS

These are the 10 strongest long-form investigation topics for YourBoyJevoy — the ones that have the most narrative potential, the clearest gap to investigate, and the most resonance with the specific audience (faith-curious, culturally honest, creative souls).

Each one is a genuine investigation, not a personal confession. You're not exposing yourself. You're investigating a pattern you've observed — in culture, in community, in yourself.

1. **"Why Do Christians Talk About Honesty But Avoid Honest Conversations?"**
   The gap between the stated value (truth) and the cultural practice (conflict avoidance, performative peace, silence as virtue). Grounded in communication psychology and faith community research.

2. **"What Sheltering Actually Does to Kids (It's Not What You Think)"**
   The gap between the intention (protection) and the outcome (secrecy, shame, unpreparedness). Grounded in developmental psychology and attachment theory.

3. **"Why We Call Avoidance 'Peace'"**
   The gap between genuine peace (resolved conflict, honest relationship) and performed peace (silence, distance, managed surfaces). Grounded in conflict resolution research and cultural patterns.

4. **"The Real Reason People Stay in Bad Situations Longer Than They Should"**
   The gap between "I would never let that happen to me" and what actually happens when you're in it. Grounded in sunk cost psychology, identity investment, and community pressure.

5. **"Why Confidence Looks Like Wisdom But Isn't"**
   The gap between the performance of certainty and the practice of discernment. Grounded in cognitive psychology and the Dunning-Kruger effect, but explored through personal observation.

6. **"What the Church Gets Wrong About Shame (And Why It Matters)"**
   The gap between shame as a tool for behavior change (what many churches practice) and shame as a barrier to genuine transformation (what the research shows). Grounded in Brené Brown's shame research and theological reflection.

7. **"Why Immigrant Families Are So Good at Surviving and So Bad at Thriving"**
   The gap between the survival skills that get you through hard times and the relational skills that let you actually enjoy what you've built. Personal, cultural, and psychological.

8. **"Why We Perform for the People Who Already Know Us"**
   The gap between who you are with strangers (often more honest) and who you are with family (often more performed). Grounded in social psychology and identity research.

9. **"The Problem With 'I'm Fine'"**
   The gap between the stated emotional state and the actual emotional state. Why we say we're fine when we're not, what it costs us, and what it would take to stop.

10. **"Why Hard Conversations Feel Like Attacks (Even When They're Not)"**
    The gap between the intent of honest feedback (love, care, investment) and the experience of receiving it (threat, criticism, rejection). Grounded in attachment theory and communication psychology.

## PART IX: HOW THE THREE LANES FEED EACH OTHER

This is the ecosystem play. Each lane builds an audience that the other lanes benefit from — without the audiences being the same people.

**The flow:**
- YourBoyJevoy attracts people who are culturally honest, faith-curious, and appreciate raw observation. Some of them will be business owners who eventually find Palmer House. Some of them will be entrepreneurs who eventually find Jevoy Palmer.
- Palmer House attracts business owners who want to use video. Some of them will be curious about the person behind the investigation and find YourBoyJevoy. Some of them will want strategic consulting and find Jevoy Palmer.
- Jevoy Palmer attracts founders and entrepreneurs who think in systems. Some of them will want video production and find Palmer House. Some of them will want the personal, creative side and find YourBoyJevoy.

**The cross-pollination rule:**
Each piece of content can *reference* the other lanes but can't *become* the other lanes. A YourBoyJevoy short can end with "if you want to go deeper on this, I explore it differently over on the Palmer House channel" — but the short itself has to be fully YourBoyJevoy. It can't be a Palmer House ad dressed up as personal content.

**The audience trust principle:**
Each audience trusts you for a specific thing. YourBoyJevoy's audience trusts you to be honest about the gap. Palmer House's audience trusts you to investigate the psychology of visibility. Jevoy Palmer's audience trusts you to think clearly about building things. Don't betray that trust by using one lane to sell another. Let the work speak. The audience will find the other lanes on their own.

## PART X: THE FAITH INTEGRATION GUIDE FOR YOURBOYJEVOY

YourBoyJevoy is where faith shows up most openly — but it still has to arrive naturally, not as a sermon. The rule from your voice calibration: **faith arrives last, quietly, as a reframe.**

In the context of YourBoyJevoy's thesis (the gap between what people say and what they do), faith integration sounds like:

- "And I think that's why the whole 'love your neighbor' thing is harder than it sounds. Because loving your neighbor means having the conversation they don't want to have. Not the one that makes everyone comfortable."
- "God isn't scared of truth. And I don't want my children to be either."
- "There's something in Proverbs about speaking truth to someone you love. It's not comfortable. But it's the most loving thing you can do."
- "I keep coming back to the idea that we were made in the image of something that doesn't hide. And yet we spend so much of our lives hiding."

**What faith integration is NOT in YourBoyJevoy:**
- A Bible verse dropped at the beginning as a credential
- A call to salvation at the end of a cultural commentary video
- Judgment of people who don't share the faith
- Using faith as a shield against honest investigation ("but God says…" as a conversation stopper)

**What faith integration IS:**
- A reframe that lands after the investigation is complete
- A question that opens rather than closes
- A personal reflection on how faith shapes the way you see the gap
- Gratitude, wonder, and spiritual honesty woven into the observation

Lane 3 · Personal Investigation

YourBoyJevoy — Something on My Mind

The shy creator's content engine. Ten ready-to-film 60–90s shorts. Conversational, observational, honest. Filmed wherever you are — kitchen, car, porch, walking. The rawness is the format.

YourBoyJevoy is a creative investigation into the gap between what people say and what people actually do — in faith, family, culture, and identity. Hosted by someone who lives at the intersection of all four.

The ramp-up plan

Months 1–3
Stage 1 — Shorts
"Something on My Mind." 60–90s. 2–3 per week. The format you're filming RIGHT NOW. Zero polish required.
Months 4–6
Stage 2 — Medium-form
"I've Been Thinking About This." 3–5 min. One observation, multiple angles. Once per week, shorts continue alongside.
Months 7+
Stage 3 — Long-form Investigation
10–15 min. Full investigative treatment. Once every 2–3 weeks. By now the camera is no longer foreign.

Format spec — "Something on My Mind"

Length: 60–90s. Hard cap at 2 min.
Structure: Name the gap → one specific example → why it matters.
Opener: "I'm not even gonna lie — something on my mind is…" (the opener is the brand)
Closer: End when the thought is complete. "That's it. That's the thought." is fine.
Polish: Zero. Real environment, real lighting, real you.
Cadence: 2–3 per week. Don't double up if you miss a day.

Faith integration rule

Faith arrives last, quietly, as a reframe. Never a Bible verse as credential. Never a call to salvation at the end of cultural commentary. Never judgment of people who don't share the faith. Never faith as a shield against honest investigation.

Examples that work: "I keep coming back to the idea that we were made in the image of something that doesn't hide. And yet we spend so much of our lives hiding."

The Umbrella

The Recorded Animal

An inquiry into what happens to human beings when our every moment becomes potentially permanent — from the campfire to the cloud.

The work you've already started has a bigger container than you've been treating it. The camera/visibility scripts are not twelve essays about content strategy. They are early chapters of a much larger investigation, and the investigation has a name: The Recorded Animal.

Everything you've already written is an early chapter inside it. Everything you're about to make can ladder up to it. The through-line — what does recording do to us — is wide enough to hold ten years of work without ever repeating itself.

Three positions almost no one else is occupying that you can:

  • Treat recording as a deeply human (not just technological) story. Most coverage stays in tech and therapy. You hold psychology, theology, anthropology, film history, business, and personal narrative in the same frame.
  • Embrace the duality. Most commentary is doom or utopia. You let the camera be a savior in one story and a destroyer in the next, without collapsing the contradiction.
  • Pull the camera back to the campfire. Almost no one connects modern visibility behavior to ancestral storytelling instincts. That through-line is your moat.

    Research

    Stories worth investigating

    Real, named, verifiable starting points. Confirm details yourself before they hit a script.

    Survival via camera / journal

    Camera saved
    Aron Ralston · Blue John Canyon, 2003
    5.5 days trapped under a boulder. Filmed messages to family on a Hi-8 camcorder. Self-amputated. Walked out alive. Start: Between a Rock and a Hard Place (2004) + his TED Talk.
    Counter-example
    José Salvador Alvarenga · 14 months at sea, 2014 rescue
    Survived without any media of his own existence. Rich contrast to Ralston — what does survival look like without the lens?
    Parallel
    Yossi Ghinsberg · Bolivian Amazon, 1981
    Lost 3 weeks. Wrote his way through it. Pre-camera, but survival-by-narration is the same instinct. Source: Jungle.
    Endurance
    Mauro Prosperi · Sahara, 1994
    Italian marathoner lost 9 days. Drank his own urine. Crossed into Algeria alive. Did video work afterward.

    The camera as instrument or witness

    Tragedy
    Timothy Treadwell · Grizzly Man, 2003
    Filmed 13 summers with grizzlies. Killed October 2003. Audio of attack exists; Herzog refused to use it. The camera as barrier between Treadwell and his own danger.
    9/11
    The 9/11 Cameramen
    Multiple operators have spoken about consciously turning cameras away when bodies began falling. Source: Jules and Gédéon Naudet's 9/11 + interviews.
    Hard episode
    Donald Crowhurst · 1968–69
    Sunday Times Golden Globe Race. Falsified logs, likely jumped overboard. Extensive tapes of his unraveling. Documentary: Deep Water (2006).
    Performance + crime
    The Capitol Self-Documenters · Jan 6 2021
    Rioters who livestreamed their own crimes. Performance instinct beating survival instinct. Source: FBI's publicly searchable archive; Atlantic + New Yorker long-forms.

    Research foundations

    Science
    Snapchat Dysmorphia · JAMA, 2018
    Rajanala, Maymone, Vashi. JAMA Facial Plastic Surgery, August 2018. Patients asking surgeons to make them look like their filtered selves. Coined by Dr. Tijion Esho.
    Data
    Selfie Death Studies · 2018+
    Bansal et al., "Selfies: A boon or bane?" — Journal of Family Medicine and Primary Care, 2018. Counts exceed 250+ from 2011–2017 alone. Carnegie Mellon tracks separately.
    Sociology
    The Looking-Glass Self · Cooley, 1902
    Charles Cooley. The original argument that the self is built from imagined reflections. Predates the camera entirely. Foundational.
    Behavior
    Bystander Effect + Cameras
    Manning, Levine, Collins on the "bystander effect myth" complicating the Kitty Genovese (1964) narrative. In the camera era, recording has partially replaced helping.

    Craft

    The investigative method

    House style for this body of work. The method matters as much as the topics. Lift this into the top of any working script file.

    • Find the specific moment first. Not the concept. The moment. A clip, a story, a single image, a single line. Build outward from there.
    • Name the mechanism. Bring the named research, the dated study, the cited thinker. This is what separates an investigation from a hot take.
    • Honor the counterargument. Always. The skeptic gets dignity before they get dismantled.
    • Refuse the easy moral. Hold the contradiction. Show the camera as savior in one frame and weapon in the next. Resist resolution.
    • End on a question, not a slogan. The body of work compounds when the audience leaves each episode with a question they can't shake.
    • Use objects. Always at least one prop that argues. Never a prop that only decorates.
    • Verse capstone. Keep it. It's the load-bearing wall of the voice.

    The Jevoy Script Formula (per-script template)

    Every script ladders through these seven beats. Adjust voice and CTA for venture, but the chassis stays.

    1. Felt moment — start with something painfully recognizable.
    2. Name the mechanism — psychology, science, research with citation.
    3. Show the object — prop, scene, metaphor, demonstration.
    4. Admit the counterargument — give the skeptic full marks first.
    5. Flip the frame — the core insight reframe.
    6. Make it practical — what this means for you.
    7. CTA + verse — venture-appropriate close, Bible verse capstone.

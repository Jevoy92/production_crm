# How to Control Your Narrative Before Someone Else Does

[VISUAL: Wide shot. Jevoy is standing in a minimalist studio space. There is a blank canvas on an easel to his left, and an empty picture frame leaning against a stool to his right. He is holding a single, unlit match. He looks at the match, then up at the camera.]

## Chapter 1: The Felt Moment

I was sitting at a dinner table a few weeks ago with a guy who is brilliant at what he does. He’s built a business that actually helps people. He’s got the expertise, he’s got the heart, and he’s got the work ethic. But he was frustrated. He looked at me and said, "Jevoy, I feel like people just don't get it. They think I'm just another consultant. They think I'm just selling a course. They don't see the actual value of what I'm building."

And I asked him a very simple question. I said, "Bro, what have you given them to look at?"

He paused. He said, "Well, my website is up. I post on LinkedIn sometimes."

I said, "No, what have you *given* them to look at? What evidence have you provided that tells them who you are, what you value, and why you are different from the noise?"

He didn't have an answer. And that’s when it hit me. We spend so much time frustrated that people misunderstand us, that the market misjudges us, that our clients don't see our full value. But we are leaving the canvas completely blank and getting mad at the picture they paint.

[VISUAL: Jevoy walks over to the empty picture frame and picks it up. He holds it in front of his face, looking through it.]

If you don't put anything in the frame, people will. They have to. It is human nature to fill a void with an assumption. If you are quiet, if you are vague, if you are hiding behind a logo and a mission statement that sounds like it was written by a committee in 2014, you are not protecting your peace. You are surrendering your narrative.

I want to investigate this today. Because I am building things right now—Palmer House Productions, my own personal brand, other ventures—and I am realizing in real time that if I don't tell you who I am, the internet will decide for me. And the internet is a terrible biographer.

It will take your worst moment, your quietest moment, or your most confusing moment, and it will make that the headline. It doesn't care about nuance. It doesn't care about your intentions. It only cares about what is visible. And if you aren't providing the visible evidence of your value, someone else is going to provide the visible evidence of your irrelevance.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism of Perception

Let’s talk about how perception actually works. There is a concept in psychology called narrative identity theory. It basically says that we make sense of the world, and of each other, by constructing stories. We don't process raw data very well. We process narratives.

When someone encounters you or your business for the first time, their brain is desperately trying to categorize you. *Is this person safe? Is this person competent? Is this person like me? Is this person full of it?*

They are looking for clues. And if you don't provide intentional clues, they will use whatever accidental clues they can find. They will look at the lighting in your Zoom call. They will look at the typo in your bio. They will look at the fact that your last Instagram post was from 2021.

[VISUAL: Jevoy sets the frame down and walks toward the camera, closing the distance. The lighting shifts slightly, becoming more intimate.]

This is the mechanism of perception: Nature abhors a vacuum, and so does human judgment.

We think that if we just put our heads down and do good work, the work will speak for itself. Come on now. You feel me? We’ve all believed that lie. "The work speaks for itself." No, it doesn't. The work is mute. The work needs a translator. The work needs a voice.

If you are a master carpenter, the table you built is beautiful. But if you don't tell me *why* you chose that wood, *how* you joined those corners without nails, and *what* that table is meant to hold, I might just think it's a nice piece of furniture. I won't know it's a legacy.

You have to control the narrative. And by control, I don't mean manipulate. I don't mean spin. I mean you have to take responsibility for the translation of your value.

Think about framing effects. Tversky and Kahneman showed us that how information is presented completely changes how it is received. If I tell you a surgery has a 90% survival rate, you feel good about it. If I tell you it has a 10% mortality rate, you are terrified. It is the exact same data. The only difference is the frame.

You are the data. Your business is the data. Your expertise is the data. But who is building the frame? If you aren't building it, the market is building it for you, and I promise you, the market's frame is not designed to make you look good. It is designed to make you look expendable.

## Chapter 3: The Object Lesson

[VISUAL: Jevoy walks over to the blank canvas on the easel. He picks up a thick black marker.]

Let me show you what I mean.

[VISUAL: Jevoy draws a single, jagged line across the canvas. It means nothing. It’s just a scribble.]

If I leave this here, and I walk out of the room, and ten people walk in, what are they going to say about this?

One person will say, "It's modern art. It represents the chaos of the human condition."
Another person will say, "A kid got loose with a Sharpie."
Another person will say, "It's a graph showing a market crash."

They are all guessing. Why? Because I gave them a data point without a narrative.

[VISUAL: Jevoy writes the word "HEARTBEAT" above the jagged line, and draws a small heart next to it.]

Now what is it? It's an EKG. It's life. It's a pulse.

The line didn't change. The context changed. The narrative changed.

Narrative is like wet cement. When you launch a business, when you step into a new role, when you start putting yourself out there, the cement is wet. You have a very short window to leave your imprint. If you don't step into it intentionally, a stray dog is going to walk across it, a leaf is going to fall in it, and it’s going to dry with a bunch of random marks that you didn't choose.

You have to leave your imprint early. You have to tell people how to read the line.

And this isn't just about marketing. This is about impression management. Erving Goffman talked about this back in the 50s. We are all performing, all the time. The question isn't whether you are performing; the question is whether your performance is an accurate reflection of your reality. Are you giving people the right cues to understand who you actually are?

If you are a deep thinker, but all you post are 15-second trending dances, you are giving them the wrong cues. If you are a premium service provider, but your website looks like it was built on a dial-up connection, you are giving them the wrong cues. You are drawing the jagged line and letting them guess what it means.

## Chapter 4: The Skeptic

Now, I know what some of you are thinking. I can hear the skeptic in the room. And it’s a fair pushback.

The skeptic says, "Jevoy, this sounds exhausting. It sounds like I have to perform all the time. It sounds like I have to curate this perfect, polished version of myself and my business, and that feels fake. I don't want to be a brand. I just want to be a person doing good work. Controlling the narrative sounds like manipulation."

I hear that. I really do. Because I hate the polished, plastic version of the internet just as much as you do. I hate the hustle-bro videos where the guy is yelling at me from a rented Lamborghini. That is manipulation. That is trying to control a narrative by faking the evidence.

But let’s give the skeptic some dignity here. The fear of being fake is a valid fear. The desire to be authentic is a noble desire.

But here is where the skeptic gets it wrong.

Authenticity is not the absence of intentionality. Let me say that again. Authenticity is not the absence of intentionality.

Just because you are being intentional about what you show people doesn't mean you are lying to them. If I invite you to my house for dinner, I am going to clean the kitchen. I am going to put the good plates out. I am going to light a candle. Am I being fake? No. I am being hospitable. I am curating an environment so that we can have a good experience together.

Controlling your narrative is not about creating a fake persona. It is about curating the truth so that it can be understood clearly. It is about removing the friction between your actual value and the market's perception of your value.

If you are a brilliant strategist, but you communicate like a disorganized mess, you aren't being authentic. You are just being lazy. Authenticity is doing the hard work of translating your internal brilliance into an external format that other people can actually consume.

## Chapter 5: The Flip

[VISUAL: The lighting shifts. A warm, subtle spotlight hits Jevoy. The background fades slightly into shadow. A very low, ambient musical pad begins to swell underneath his voice—barely noticeable at first, just adding emotional weight.]

Here is the flip. Here is the moment we have to change how we see this.

We think controlling the narrative is an act of ego. We think it’s about shouting, "Look at me! Look how great I am! Look at my business!"

But what if controlling your narrative is actually an act of service?

Think about it. If you have a solution that can genuinely help someone—if you can save them time, if you can ease their anxiety, if you can build them a better system, if you can capture their story in a way that honors them—and they don't hire you because they misunderstood what you do... who loses?

They do.

They lose because you were too afraid of looking arrogant to be clear. They lose because you left the canvas blank and let them guess.

When you take control of your narrative, you are doing the heavy lifting for your audience. You are saying, "I know you are busy. I know you are overwhelmed with choices. I am not going to make you guess what I do or why I matter. I am going to hand you the map. I am going to give you the translation."

It is not ego to be clear. It is empathy.

I am learning this right now with Palmer House Productions. We are a translation company that happens to own cameras. That’s the narrative. If I just say, "We make videos," I am leaving the canvas blank. Everyone makes videos. Your nephew with an iPhone makes videos. But if I say, "We help businesses become easier to understand, trust, and choose through strategic cinematic video," I have drawn the heartbeat over the line. I have given you the context.

And it’s the same with my personal brand. If I just say, "I'm an entrepreneur," that means nothing. But if I say, "I am a business architect who builds systems that scale," now you know exactly where to put me in your mental filing cabinet. I have served you by being clear.

## Chapter 6: The Practical Application

So, how do we actually do this? How do we control the narrative before someone else does, without losing our soul in the process?

I want to give you a framework. Because I am a systems guy, and ideas without architecture are just wishes.

If you want to control your narrative, you have to answer three questions, and you have to answer them publicly, repeatedly, and clearly.

**Question 1: What is the problem you actually solve?**
Not the service you provide. The problem you solve. People don't buy services; they buy solutions to their pain. If you are a financial advisor, you don't sell wealth management. You sell the ability to sleep through the night during a recession. You have to articulate the problem better than your client can. When you describe their pain accurately, they automatically assume you hold the cure.

**Question 2: What is your unique mechanism?**
How do you solve the problem differently than the next guy? This is where you show your work. This is where you pull back the curtain. Don't just say you are "innovative" or "dedicated." Show me the blueprint. Show me the framework. For me, it’s the fact that I look at video as a business system, not just a creative project. What is your mechanism? Give it a name. Make it tangible.

**Question 3: What do you stand against?**
This is the most powerful one. A narrative is defined just as much by what it rejects as by what it embraces. What is the industry standard that you refuse to accept? What is the common practice that you think is garbage? When you plant a flag and say, "We don't do it that way," you instantly attract the people who are tired of "that way."

You take those three answers, and you build your evidence. You make the videos. You write the articles. You record the podcasts. You don't wait for someone to ask you; you put the evidence in the room before they even walk in.

You have to become the author of your own story. Because if you don't write it, the market will write it for you, and the market is a terrible writer. It relies on clichés. It relies on stereotypes. It relies on whatever is easiest to digest. You have to give them something better to chew on.

## Chapter 7: The Insight and The Invitation

We are all building something. A business, a family, a legacy, a life.

And the truth is, you cannot control what people think of you. You can't. People will project their own insecurities, their own biases, and their own history onto you. That is out of your hands.

But you can control what you give them to look at.

You can control the evidence. You can control the translation. You can refuse to leave the canvas blank.

Because here is the deepest truth about who you are and what you are building. You were not made to be a blank slate for other people's assumptions. You were made with intention. You were made with a specific design, a specific voice, and a specific purpose.

There is a verse in Genesis, right at the beginning of the story. Genesis 1:27. It says, "So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them."

Think about what that means for a second. It means you are an image-bearer. You are a reflection of something intentional, something creative, something deeply valuable. You have inherent dignity, not because of what you produce, but because of what you are.

And if you carry that kind of value, it is a tragedy to let the world misunderstand it because you were too quiet to explain it.

Don't let the internet write your biography. Don't let the market guess your value. Pick up the pen. Pick up the camera. Tell the truth about what you are building, and tell it so clearly that they can't help but understand.

If that question lives inside you too—if you are trying to figure out how to translate your value to the world—stay close. There's more coming.

[VISUAL: Jevoy looks at the camera for a long beat. He gives a slight nod. Cut to black.]

# Theme 5: How to Control Your Narrative Before Someone Else Does (MindYourBizniz Version)

[COLD OPEN]

[ROOM TONE — quiet, intimate space. The sound of a ceramic mug being set down on a wooden table.]

I want to talk about something. 

[BEAT]

Actually, I want to talk about the thing we all try *not* to talk about when we’re building something. The quiet anxiety that sits in the back of your throat when you realize that people are looking at you, and you have absolutely no idea what they’re seeing. 

I was sitting with a friend the other day. Brilliant guy. He’s building a business that actually matters—not just another widget, not just another SaaS product that nobody needs, but something that solves a real, tangible problem for real people. And he was frustrated. He was exhausted by the game of it all. He looked at me across the table, shook his head, and said, "Jevoy, I just want the work to speak for itself. I don't want to play the game. I don't want to be a dancing monkey on the internet. I don't want to have to constantly explain myself. If the product is good, if the service is excellent, people will figure it out."

And I looked at him, and I said, "Bro. Come on now. Since when has the world ever just 'figured it out'?"

[PAUSE — 3 seconds]

Here’s the truth we don’t want to admit, the truth that makes us uncomfortable because it requires us to take responsibility for something we’d rather leave to chance: The work never speaks for itself. The work is mute. *You* have to speak for the work. And if you don't, someone else will. Or worse, their assumptions will. 

Welcome to MindYourBizniz. Let me sit with this for a minute.

[MUSIC FADES IN — a low, warm, sustained pad. Not distracting, just holding space.]

## Chapter 1: The Felt Moment

You know that feeling when you walk into a room, and you can tell people were just talking about you? The air is a little too still. The smiles are a little too quick. The conversation shifts just a fraction of a second too late. 

That’s what it feels like to have a brand without a narrative. You know people are perceiving you, you know they are forming opinions and making judgments, but you have absolutely no control over the lens they’re using. You are a character in a story they are writing, and you haven't even seen the script.

We all want to believe that if we just put our heads down and do good work, the market will reward us with accurate understanding. We think, "I’m a good person, I have a good service, I treat my clients well, therefore, people will naturally see me as a good option." We treat the market like it's a perfectly rational, perfectly observant judge that weighs all the evidence fairly before coming to a conclusion.

But that’s not how human psychology works. We are meaning-making machines. If you give a human being a blank space, they will not leave it blank. They will fill it with their own biases, their own past experiences, their own fears, and their own insecurities. 

If you don't tell people who you are, they will decide who you are based on the smallest, most random pieces of information they can find. A typo on your website. The fact that you didn't respond to an email fast enough. The way your logo looks on a mobile phone. The tone of voice you used in a single, rushed interaction. 

You are being judged right now. Every time someone lands on your page, every time someone hears your name, a judgment is being formed. The only question is: Are you giving them the evidence to judge you accurately? Or are you leaving them to guess?

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism of Perception

Let’s talk about how this actually works in the brain, because this isn't just about marketing; this is about human cognition. There’s a concept in psychology called narrative identity theory. It basically says that we don’t just experience life as a series of random, disconnected events; we organize our lives into stories to make sense of them. We need a beginning, a middle, and an end. We need heroes and villains. We need cause and effect.

And we do the exact same thing with other people, and with businesses. 

When someone encounters your brand for the first time, their brain is desperately trying to categorize you. It takes a massive amount of cognitive energy to evaluate something entirely new, so the brain looks for shortcuts. *Is this person safe? Is this business competent? Are they expensive? Are they cheap? Are they for me, or are they for someone else? Do they remind me of that guy who ripped me off five years ago, or do they remind me of the mentor who changed my life?*

They are looking for a frame. 

Think about framing effects. This is classic behavioral economics. If I tell you a surgery has a 90% survival rate, you feel pretty good about it. You think, "Okay, those are solid odds. I can do this." If I tell you it has a 10% mortality rate, you’re terrified. You think, "I have a one in ten chance of dying on that table." It’s the exact same data. The exact same reality. The only difference is the frame. 

When you refuse to control your narrative—when you say, "I just want the work to speak for itself"—you are handing the frame over to the market. You are letting them decide whether you are the 90% survival rate or the 10% mortality rate. 

And let me tell you something about the market: The market is tired. The market is distracted. The market has been burned before. The market defaults to skepticism. If you don't give them a clear, compelling reason to trust you, they will default to doubt. They will assume the worst because assuming the worst is a survival mechanism. 

## Chapter 3: The Object in Mind

I want you to picture something with me. Just close your eyes if you’re not driving. 

Picture an empty picture frame. A beautiful, ornate, heavy wooden frame. The kind you’d see in a museum holding a masterpiece. But there’s no canvas inside it. Just the blank wall behind it. 

If you hang that empty frame in a gallery, people will stand in front of it and try to figure out what it means. They won't just walk past it; they will stop and analyze it. They’ll look at the texture of the wall. They’ll look at the shadow the frame casts. They’ll invent a story about why the artist left it blank. 

*Oh, it’s a commentary on the emptiness of modern life.*
*No, it’s about the invisible labor of the working class.*
*Actually, I think it represents the void of the digital age.*

They will put something in the frame. They cannot help themselves.

Your business is that frame. Your personal brand is that frame. If you don't put a clear, intentional picture inside it, the market will stare at the blank wall and invent a story. And I promise you, the story they invent will not be as good as the truth. It will be a story based on their own limitations, not your potential.

Narrative is like wet cement. When you first launch, when you first meet someone, when you first introduce an idea to the world, the cement is wet. You can draw in it. You can shape it. You can write your name in it. But if you just stand there and do nothing, the cement is going to dry. And whatever leaves, twigs, or random footprints fell into it while you were waiting for the work to speak for itself? That’s your narrative now. That is what hardens into reality.

If you don't leave your own imprint early, something else will. And once that cement dries, it takes a jackhammer to change it.

## Chapter 4: The Friend Who Pushes Back

Now, I know what some of you are thinking. Because I have a friend who pushes back on this every time we talk. He’s the guy who always keeps me honest.

He says, "Jevoy, this all sounds like spin. It sounds like manipulation. You’re telling me to manufacture a persona. I don't want to be one of those fake internet gurus who curates every single second of their life to look perfect. I don't want to rent a Lamborghini and pretend it's mine. I just want to be authentic. I just want to be real."

And I hear that. I really do. Because I hate the fake stuff too. You feel me? I can’t stand the overproduced, sterile, perfectly polished versions of reality that we see online. I can't stand the people who claim authority they haven't earned, who talk about their 'hundreds of clients' when they've only ever worked with their cousin. 

But here is where my friend gets it wrong. Here is where the desire for authenticity becomes an excuse for poor communication.

Controlling your narrative is not about spin. It’s not about manipulation. It’s about translation. 

You are not inventing a fake story. You are taking the very real, very messy, very beautiful truth of what you do, and you are translating it into a language that other people can understand. 

Authenticity without clarity is just noise. You can be the most authentic person in the world, you can have the purest intentions, but if you are speaking a language the market doesn't understand, you are still going to be misunderstood. 

We are not talking about creating a false image. We are talking about providing accurate evidence. We are talking about making sure that the outside matches the inside. If you are a professional, your narrative should communicate professionalism. If you are deeply empathetic, your narrative should communicate empathy. If you are a systems thinker, your narrative should communicate structure. 

If you don't translate your internal reality into an external narrative, you are hoarding your value.

## Chapter 5: The Flip

[MUSIC SWELLS SLIGHTLY — the pad deepens, becoming more resonant, filling the space.]

And this is the flip. This is the moment where the whole paradigm shifts. This is where the architecture of the room gets built quietly.

Stop thinking about marketing as "selling." Stop thinking about content as "promotion." Stop thinking about narrative control as some kind of dark art.

Start thinking about it as evidence. 

You are a lawyer in the courtroom of public opinion, and your job is to present the evidence that proves your value. The jury is not going to go out and investigate the crime scene for you. You have to bring the evidence to them.

If you are a good person, prove it. 
If your service changes lives, prove it. 
If you care about your clients more than the competition does, prove it. 

You don't prove it by saying, "I care." Everybody says they care. The worst companies in the world have "we care about our customers" written on their websites. You prove it by showing the receipts. You prove it by telling the stories. You prove it by letting people see the way you think, the way you operate, the way you treat people when things go wrong. 

When you control your narrative, you are simply taking the truth and putting a spotlight on it. You are saying, "Look here. Don't look at the blank wall. Look at this."

You are not manipulating them. You are saving them the cognitive energy of having to guess who you are. You are giving them the gift of clarity. You are making it easy for the right people to say yes, and easy for the wrong people to say no. 

## Chapter 6: The Emotional Part

[MUSIC SHIFTS — warmer, more intimate, stripping away the layers until it's just a single, resonant tone.]

Let’s get real for a second. Let's talk about what's really going on underneath all of this. The reason most of us don't want to control our narrative isn't because we’re afraid of being manipulative. It's not because we're so fiercely dedicated to authenticity.

It’s because we’re afraid of being seen. 

It is terrifying to stand up and say, "This is who I am, and this is what I believe." Because the moment you define yourself, you give people the opportunity to reject you. 

If you stay vague, if you stay quiet, if you just let the work "speak for itself," you can always hide behind the ambiguity. If someone doesn't buy from you, you can tell yourself, "Well, they just didn't get it. They didn't understand the nuance of what I do." You can protect your ego.

But if you put your narrative out there clearly—if you say, "I am building this, I believe in this, and this is the value I bring"—and they still walk away? That hurts. That feels like a rejection of *you*. 

I’m building these ventures right now. Palmer House, beSettld, the personal brand. And I’m starting from zero. I don't have hundreds of clients. I have a handful of people who trusted me early. I am in the launch phase. And every time I hit publish, every time I put a piece of the narrative out there, there is a voice in my head that says, "Who do you think you are? You haven't earned the right to speak yet. You should wait until you're bigger. You should wait until you're more successful."

But then I remember the empty frame. 

I remember that if I don't tell the story of what I’m building, someone else will look at my small beginnings and call it a failure. They will look at my launch phase and call it amateur. They will look at my process and call it disorganized.

I have to tell them the story of the foundation. I have to say, "We are building the architecture right now. The room gets built quietly. You are watching the scaffolding go up." 

You have to have the courage to tell the truth about where you are, before someone else tells a lie about where you’re going. You have to be willing to be seen in your current state, without inflating it, but also without apologizing for it. 

## Chapter 7: Invitation and Verse

[MUSIC FADES OUT SLOWLY, leaving just the resonance of the last chord.]

[ROOM TONE RETURNS — the quiet intimacy of the space.]

So, what is the narrative you are leaving to chance? What is the empty frame you are letting the market fill with their own assumptions? Where are you letting the cement dry while you wait for the work to speak for itself?

It’s time to pick up the brush. It’s time to provide the evidence. It's time to translate your internal reality into an external truth that people can actually understand.

If that’s the kind of conversation you’ve been needing, stay close. There’s more coming. We are building the room, and there is a seat at the table for you.

[PAUSE — 2 seconds]

I was reading recently, and I was reminded of this idea from the very beginning of the book. Genesis 1:27. It talks about how humanity was created in the image of the Creator. 

Think about what that means. To bear an image. It means you are a reflection of something intentional. You are not a random collection of atoms; you are a deliberate design. You have inherent dignity, not because of what you produce, not because of how much money you make, but because of whose image you bear. 

When you tell your story accurately, when you refuse to let the world define you with its broken lenses and its cynical assumptions, you are honoring that original design. You are saying, "I will not let my identity be reduced to an assumption. I will reflect the truth. I will stand in the light and let myself be seen accurately."

[BEAT]

Thanks for being here. MindYourBizniz is a Palmer House conversation. New episode next week.

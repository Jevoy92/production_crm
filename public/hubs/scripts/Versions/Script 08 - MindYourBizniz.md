# Why Clarity Is More Powerful Than Creativity

[COLD OPEN]

[ROOM TONE — 3 seconds of quiet, ambient space]

[SFX: A ceramic coffee mug is set down gently on a wooden desk]

[PAUSE — 2 seconds]

I want to talk about something. 

Something that I see quietly exhausting a lot of people right now. Especially if you are building something. Especially if you are a founder, a creator, or just someone trying to put your work out into the world and hoping that the right people actually notice it. 

I was sitting in my office the other day, just scrolling through my feed, looking at how different businesses and brands present themselves. And I noticed this pattern. It’s a pattern I’ve fallen into myself, so I recognize the texture of it. I saw video after video, post after post, website after website, where you could feel the sheer amount of effort radiating off the screen. You could feel the sweat. The agonizing over the perfect transition. The obsession with the exact right color grade. The attempt to sound profound, poetic, and deeply innovative. 

It was all very clever. It was all very creative. 

But after spending two full minutes looking at a company’s page, I realized something terrifying: I had absolutely no idea what they actually did. 

I didn't know what problem they solved. I didn't know who they were for. I didn't know how to hire them, and I didn't know why I should. They had spent all their energy trying to be admired for their creativity, and they had completely forgotten to be understood. 

And bro, I get it. I really do. When you care about your work, you want it to look beautiful. You want it to feel cinematic. You want people to look at what you’ve built and think, *Wow, that’s genius.* But here is the quiet, uncomfortable truth that we have to sit with today: Clever gets admired. Clear gets acted on. 

We are living in an era where everyone is trying to out-create each other. The barrier to entry for making something look good has never been lower. Anyone can get a template. Anyone can use AI to write a poetic caption. Anyone can throw a trending audio track under a slow-motion video. The internet is flooded with beautiful, confusing noise. 

And in the middle of all that noise, the most radical, disruptive thing you can do is just be incredibly, unapologetically clear.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism of Understanding

[ROOM TONE]

Let’s break down why this happens. Because it’s not just a mistake; it’s a psychological trap. And if we don’t understand the mechanics of the trap, we’re going to keep walking right into it. 

There is a concept in psychology called "processing fluency." It was heavily researched by psychologists like Rolf Reber and Norbert Schwarz back in the late nineties. Processing fluency is essentially a measure of how easy it is for our brains to process information. And what the research shows—time and time again—is that human beings have a deep, hardwired bias toward cognitive ease. 

When something is easy to understand, our brains don't just process it faster. We actually perceive it as more true, more trustworthy, and more valuable. Let me say that again, because it is the hinge that this whole conversation swings on: We equate ease of understanding with truth and value. 

When you make your audience work hard to figure out what you are saying, you aren't just making them tired. You are actively eroding their trust in you. If they have to burn mental calories just to decode your clever metaphor, their brain starts signaling, *This is difficult. This might be risky. Let's move on.*

But there’s a second psychological force at play here, and it’s the reason why you and I struggle with this so much. It’s called the "curse of knowledge." This was coined by economists Colin Camerer, George Loewenstein, and Martin Weber in 1989. The curse of knowledge is a cognitive bias that occurs when an individual, communicating with other individuals, unknowingly assumes that the others have the background to understand. 

You know your business inside and out. You know the industry jargon. You know the complex, nuanced philosophy behind why you do what you do. You’ve been living in it for years. So when you sit down to write a script, or build a website, or record a podcast, your brain automatically skips over the foundational stuff because, to you, it feels obvious. It feels boring. 

You think, *I can't just say 'I help plumbers get more leads.' That's too basic. I need to say 'I architect paradigm-shifting growth ecosystems for home service professionals.'* 

Come on now. You feel me? We’ve all done it. We dress our value up in a tuxedo of complex words because we are terrified that the naked truth of what we do isn't impressive enough. We suffer from the curse of knowledge, so we skip the clarity and go straight for the poetry. 

But your audience isn't inside your head. They are standing on the outside, looking at a locked door, and instead of handing them the key, you are describing the metallurgical composition of the lock. It doesn't help them get inside.

## Chapter 3: The Object in Mind

I want you to picture something with me. Just hold this image in your mind. 

Imagine I am holding a piece of glass. It’s a thick, heavy pane of glass, but it is completely fogged over. It’s covered in condensation, maybe a little grime. If you hold it up to your face and try to look through it, you can see shapes, you can see light and shadow, but you can’t make out any details. You can’t read the sign on the other side. You can’t see the path. 

Now, imagine I take a beautiful, ornate, gold-leaf frame, and I wrap it around this fogged glass. I carve intricate designs into the wood. I embed jewels into the corners. I spend hours making the frame an absolute masterpiece of creativity. 

Does that help you see through the glass? 

No. It just gives you something pretty to look at while you remain completely blind to what’s on the other side. 

That is what we do when we prioritize creativity over clarity. We spend all our time building a beautiful frame around a foggy message. We obsess over the lighting, the camera angles, the font choices, the clever wordplay. But the lens itself is still dirty. 

Now, imagine I take a plain, simple rag. No gold frame. No jewels. Just a rag. And I wipe the glass completely clean. Suddenly, the fog is gone. The dirt is gone. You can see straight through it. You can see the path. You can read the sign. 

Creativity gets attention. But clarity gives people sight. 

Think about it like food. I love a good meal. I love when a chef knows exactly how to use spices to elevate a dish. But creativity is the seasoning. Clarity is the meal. And nobody—I don't care how hungry they are—nobody wants to sit down at a restaurant and be served a plate of pure paprika. 

You have to give them the meat and potatoes first. You have to give them the substance. You have to tell them exactly what they are eating before you ask them to appreciate the subtle notes of truffle oil.

## Chapter 4: The Friend Who Pushes Back

Now, I know what happens here. Because I have this conversation all the time. 

There is a friend who pushes back. Maybe it’s a literal friend, or maybe it’s just that voice in your own head. And that voice says, *“But Jevoy, creativity matters. Art matters. If I just strip everything down to the most basic, clear, functional language, I’m going to sound like everybody else. I’m going to be boring. I’m going to lose my soul in the name of marketing.”*

And I want to pause right here and give that pushback the dignity it deserves. Because it’s a valid fear. 

I am a creative. I run a production company. I care deeply about aesthetics, about storytelling, about the emotional resonance of a beautiful image. I am not sitting here telling you that art is dead and we should all just communicate in bullet points and binary code. 

The friend who pushes back is right about one thing: a world without creativity is a sterile, depressing place. If you strip all the personality, all the flavor, and all the art out of your work, you will blend in. You will become a commodity. 

But here is where the friend who pushes back gets it wrong. 

The assumption is that clarity and creativity are enemies. The assumption is that they are sitting on opposite ends of a seesaw, and if you raise one, you have to lower the other. But that’s a false dichotomy. 

Creativity and clarity are not enemies. They are partners. But they have a very specific order of operations. 

Creativity should serve understanding, not replace it. 

When you use creativity to obscure your message—when you use big words to hide the fact that you don't actually know what you're talking about, or when you use flashy visuals to distract from a weak product—that’s not art. That’s camouflage. 

True creativity, the kind that actually moves people, the kind that builds legacy, is creativity that illuminates. It’s the metaphor that makes a complex idea suddenly click in someone’s mind. It’s the visual that perfectly captures a feeling they couldn't put into words. 

You don't have to choose between being clear and being creative. But you do have to choose which one is the foundation and which one is the house. If you build a house out of pure creativity with no foundation of clarity, it will collapse the second the wind blows. 

[PAUSE — 4 seconds]

## Chapter 5: The Flip

So, how do we flip this? How do we actually change the way we operate? 

It starts with a fundamental shift in what we are trying to achieve when we communicate. 

Most of the time, when we sit down to write a post, or record a video, or design a landing page, our subconscious goal is: *I want them to think I am smart. I want them to think I am talented. I want them to be impressed by me.*

That is an ego-driven goal. And ego is the enemy of clarity. 

We have to flip the goal. The new goal is not: *I want them to be impressed by me.* The new goal is: *I want them to feel understood by me, and I want them to understand exactly how I can help them.*

When you make that flip, everything changes. You stop trying to sound like a philosopher and you start sounding like a guide. You stop using jargon and you start using the actual words your clients use when they are stressed out at 2:00 AM. 

Let me give you a real example. Let’s say you are a financial advisor. The clever, creative, ego-driven way to position yourself is to say: *“I orchestrate bespoke wealth-accumulation paradigms for high-net-worth visionaries.”* 

Bro. What does that even mean? If I read that, I am scrolling past it immediately because my brain does not have the processing fluency to decode it, and I don't trust it. 

The clear way to say it is: *“I help families build wealth so they don't have to worry about money when they retire.”*

Is it poetic? No. Is it going to win a literary award? No. But it is clear. It is a clean lens. I can look right through it and see exactly what is on the other side. And once I understand that you can help me retire without fear, *then* you can use your creativity to tell me a beautiful story about what retirement could look like. 

Clarity is the hook. Creativity is the journey. 

When you prioritize clarity, you actually create a safer container for your creativity. Because once the audience knows they are in good hands, once they know exactly what the premise is, they will relax. They will trust you. And when they trust you, they will follow you into the deeper, more creative, more nuanced parts of your work.

## Chapter 6: The Emotional Part

[MUSIC FADES IN — A very subtle, warm, ambient drone. Barely there, just enough to hold the space. It stays underneath the dialogue]

I want to speak directly to the exhaustion for a minute. Because I know it’s there. 

If you have been running on the treadmill of trying to be the most clever, the most innovative, the most visually stunning brand in your space, you are probably tired. It is an exhausting game to play, because the bar keeps moving. There is always someone with a better camera. There is always someone with a sharper wit. There is always a new trend, a new audio, a new format that you are supposed to master. 

And the pressure to constantly perform at that level of creative output can paralyze you. It creates what I call the "founder's freeze." You sit down to make something, and because it isn't a cinematic masterpiece, you just don't publish it. You leave it in the drafts. You stay quiet. 

I want to offer you a massive sigh of relief today. 

You do not have to be a genius every time you speak. You do not have to reinvent the wheel every time you post. You just have to be a translator. 

Your job is to take the value that is locked inside your head, or locked inside your business, and translate it into a language that the person who needs it can easily understand. That’s it. That is the whole job. 

There is a profound humility in clarity. It requires you to step out of the spotlight and put the audience’s understanding above your own desire to look smart. It requires you to say, *“I am willing to use simple words, even if it makes me look less sophisticated, because I care more about helping you than I care about impressing you.”*

And here is the beautiful irony of it all: When you stop trying so hard to look smart, people actually start trusting your authority. 

The room gets built quietly. You don't need fireworks. You don't need a marching band. You just need to lay one clear brick after another. You just need to show up, look people in the eye, and tell them the truth in a way they can understand. 

When you do that, you stop being just another noisy brand in their feed. You become a place of rest. You become a place of cognitive ease. In a world that is constantly confusing them, you become the one person who makes sense. 

And people will always return to the place that makes sense.

## Chapter 7: The Invitation

If you are listening to this, and you are realizing that you’ve been serving plates of paprika... if you realize you’ve been building beautiful frames around fogged glass... it’s okay. We’ve all been there. 

But today is the day you take the rag and wipe the glass clean. 

Look at your website. Look at your last five posts. Look at the way you introduce yourself at a dinner party. Ask yourself, honestly: *Am I trying to be clever, or am I trying to be clear?* 

If you need to strip it down to the studs, strip it down. Find the simplest, truest sentence about what you do, and start there. 

If this is the kind of conversation you’ve been needing—if you are tired of the hacks and the trends and you just want to build something real, something that lasts, something that is grounded in truth—stay close. There is a lot more where this came from. We are just getting started. 

[PAUSE — 3 seconds]

I want to leave you with this thought. It’s an ancient piece of wisdom, written thousands of years ago, but it is the ultimate blueprint for everything we’ve talked about today. 

*“Write the vision, and make it plain upon tables, that he may run that readeth it.”*

Make it plain. Not complicated. Not obscure. Plain. So that the person who reads it doesn't have to stop and decode it. They can just run.

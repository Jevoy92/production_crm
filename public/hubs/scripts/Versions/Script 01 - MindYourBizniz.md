# SCRIPT 1 — MINDYOURBIZNIZ VERSION
## Episode: "The Camera Is a Mirror With Memory"

*Format: Solo podcast monologue · Target runtime: 14–16 min · ~2,490 words*
*Pillar: Podcast · Status: Draft V1 · Last updated: 2026-04-27*

---

[COLD OPEN — no music. Just room tone. Slight reverb. Mug being set down on a table.]

**JEVOY:**
I want to talk about something that I don't think gets talked about enough — and I want to take my time with it, because I think most of the conversations about this happen at the wrong altitude.

The conversation usually sounds like: *post more.* *Get over yourself.* *Get on camera.* *Build your personal brand.* *Be more authentic.* All those phrases that mean nothing because they've been said by everyone.

I don't want to have that conversation today.

I want to have the conversation under that one.

---

## CHAPTER 1 — THE FELT MOMENT

So here's a scene. And I want you to actually picture it because I think it'll do most of the work for me.

You're a business owner. You've built something real. Could be a service. Could be a product. Could be a practice. Doesn't matter. You've put years into it. The work is good. The clients you have are happy. Some of them have been with you for so long they feel like family. And if you sat down across from someone who needed what you do, and the two of you just *talked* — you'd close them in twenty minutes. That's not arrogance. That's just what's true. The thing you sell, the thing you do, the thing you know — it lands. In a room. With another human. It lands.

[PAUSE.]

Now picture the same week, but on Wednesday afternoon, you sit down to record a video about your business. And you've already psyched yourself up. You watched two reels by some twenty-three-year-old who never paid a payroll telling you the algorithm rewards consistency. You watched another one about the perfect hook. You bought a ring light off Amazon and it arrived with a dented box. You taped a piece of paper to the bottom of the camera with your three talking points. You sat down. You took a breath. You pressed record.

And you froze.

Not dramatically. Not visibly. You said the first line. It came out flat. You said the second line. You started to feel your face. You said the third line and you noticed your eyes weren't looking at the camera, they were looking *past* it, like there was something interesting just behind it, which there wasn't. You stopped. You watched it back. You hated it. You deleted it. You closed the laptop. You told yourself you'd come back to it tomorrow.

You didn't come back to it tomorrow.

[PAUSE — three full seconds. Let it sit.]

If you've lived inside that scene — I'm not interested in selling you anything today. I'm interested in *naming* what just happened to you. Because I think we've been getting it wrong, and the wrongness is costing people their businesses, their voice, and in some cases, their belief that what they built is worth talking about.

That's the conversation I want to have.

---

## CHAPTER 2 — THE MECHANISM

So here's what I learned, and it broke open a door for me.

There's a thing in psychology called *objective self-awareness*. Two psychologists named Shelley Duval and Robert Wicklund wrote about it in 1972 — long before any of this content era we're living in. And the basic idea is so simple it sounds obvious until you really sit with it.

Most of the time, you live inside yourself. You're the *subject*. You're the one having the experience. You don't watch yourself eat lunch. You just eat lunch.

But the *moment* something makes you aware of yourself as a *thing*, as an *object* — a mirror, an audience, the eyes of someone evaluating you, *a camera* — you flip. You stop being the subject. You become the object. You start watching yourself from outside yourself.

That flip is not a small thing. That flip is the entire game.

Because the version of you that lives inside the subject mode — that's the version of you that closes deals. That's the version of you that wins a room. That's the version of you that says the smart thing you said three years ago that someone still quotes back to you. That version is *running on instinct*. It's not monitoring. It's not editing. It's just *being*. And when it's being, it is excellent. Because you've put fifteen years into being it.

The moment the camera comes on, you're not being it anymore. You're *watching* it. From outside. And it's not as good when you're watching it. It's stiffer. It's flatter. It's making decisions on a one-second delay. You can feel it. And the feeling makes it worse, which makes you watch yourself more, which makes you stiffer, which makes you feel worse — it's a loop.

[PAUSE.]

This is not — I want to say this clearly — this is not nerves. Nerves are a part of it. But the mechanism underneath is structural. Hawthorne Works experiments in the 1920s showed the same thing in factories: people behave differently the second they're observed. Not better. Not worse. *Differently*. The watching changes the doing.

So what's happening to you when you sit down to record isn't a confidence problem. It's a *physics* problem. There's a real friction between the version of you that builds the business and the version of you that has to film a video about the business. And we've been pretending it doesn't exist.

---

## CHAPTER 3 — THE OBJECT YOU CAN SEE IN YOUR MIND

I want to give you an image to hold onto, because I think it makes the whole thing click.

Imagine two objects on a table. A handheld mirror. And a phone.

The mirror is what most people think a camera is. You look in it, you see yourself, you adjust. You smooth your hair. You straighten the collar. And then you walk away, and the mirror forgets. The mirror has no memory. The mirror is private. You and the mirror are alone.

A camera looks like a mirror. It feels like a mirror in the moment. But it isn't one. A camera is a mirror *with memory*. A camera makes a version of you that survives the room. A version of you that can be paused. Slowed. Replayed. Sent to your mother-in-law. Used in a podcast someone is making about why your industry is doomed. Watched by a procurement officer at 11pm who is deciding whether to put you on the shortlist.

That's a different object. That's not a mirror. That's an evidence locker.

And your nervous system knows. Your nervous system is doing the math, in real time, on what stakes are involved every time a lens points at you. Your nervous system isn't broken. It's *accurate*. It's correctly identifying that this is not a low-stakes moment. This is a moment that will travel.

And so it flips the safety on. It moves you out of *subject* mode and into *object* mode. It wants to protect what you built. It does so by making you wooden. It is, in a strange, slightly tragic way, *trying to help*.

And we keep yelling at it.

---

## CHAPTER 4 — THE FRIEND WHO PUSHES BACK

OK. So I can already hear the friend in this conversation pushing back on me. Because I have this friend in my head, and I think you might too, and that friend is *not wrong* about everything. So I want to give them their say. Honestly.

The friend says — *Jevoy, you are overthinking this. The successful people on the internet did not sit down and study Duval and Wicklund. They sat down and pressed record. A thousand times. And they got better at it. Because that's how you get good at anything. Reps. Stop turning every awkward iPhone video into a TED talk about identity. Just post.*

The friend is partly right.

Reps matter. Practice helps. You absolutely do get less self-conscious the more you do something. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling you something. So let's give that its full weight: practice softens the freeze. Real. True. Buy that one.

But here's what the friend is missing.

The thing you build *through* the reps is not, actually, "courage." It's a *room*. It's a set of conditions. It's a way of sitting, a way of starting, a way of resetting, a kind of self-permission that you accumulate over enough takes that it stops feeling like a performance. The reps are not building a different *you*. The reps are building a different *context* for the same you. The you that's good in a room with one human is, mechanically, the same nervous system that's locked up in front of the lens — but after enough reps, you've built a room around the lens where that nervous system can finally relax.

That's not a discipline story. That's an *architecture* story.

And the reason this matters is that a lot of people are telling themselves they don't have what it takes — when really what they don't have is *the room*. They don't have the right lighting, the right cadence, the right warm-up, the right partner, the right editor who tells them what to cut. They're sitting alone in a kitchen with a phone, trying to do something that was actually never meant to be done alone.

We blamed their courage. The problem was their setup.

So the friend is right about reps. The friend is wrong about *what the reps are doing*. And that distinction is, in my view, the difference between business owners who quietly become great on camera over two years, and business owners who quietly give up after two months.

---

## CHAPTER 5 — THE FLIP

So here's what I came around to. And I'll tell you, this reframe was the thing that changed how I talked to my own clients about this.

The camera isn't capturing you. We keep using that word. *Capture.* Like the camera is a butterfly net.

The camera is *adding* something. The camera is adding an audience.

That's it. That's the whole thing. The moment the lens is in the room, there is a new audience in the room. Invisible. Maybe future. Maybe imagined. Maybe never even going to exist. *Doesn't matter.* Your brain has added them to the room. Your behavior is adjusting for their presence. Your body is calibrating to the stakes of being seen by people who aren't currently in the building.

That is exactly what your brain *should* be doing. It is doing the job evolution gave it.

So the question — and this is the question that I think actually moves the conversation forward — is not *how do I be more authentic with the camera in the room.* The question is *which version of me do I want to send into the rooms I'll never be in, and what does that version need from me in order to show up?*

That's a real question. It has a real answer. And the answer is different for everyone.

For some of you, the version you want to send is the warm one. The version that puts people at ease. So you have to build a room that lets warmth out — slower pacing, softer light, a chair that isn't a folding chair, twenty minutes of warmup before you press record.

For others, the version you want to send is the sharp one. The version that lands the insight in eight words. So your setup looks completely different. Faster cuts. Standing up. No script.

The version is not the problem. The *match* between the version and the setup is the problem. And almost nobody is paying attention to the match.

That's the work. That's where the actual conversation lives.

---

## CHAPTER 6 — THE EMOTIONAL PART

[MUSIC FADES IN, low — quiet pad. Sits underneath the next three paragraphs.]

I want to take this somewhere I don't usually take it on this show, and then I want to come back.

A lot of the entrepreneurs I talk to — and I mean the ones whose work I genuinely respect — are not afraid of cameras the way they think they are. They're afraid of *being seen unfinished*. They're afraid of being witnessed in the middle of becoming the person they're trying to become. They're afraid that if they post the video, somebody who knew them ten years ago will see it and think, *who do you think you are?*

That's not a confidence problem. That's an identity problem.

And the camera, by being unmoved and unblinking and uncomfortably patient, exposes it in a way that almost nothing else does. The camera says, *show me who you are right now*, and a lot of us realize we have been carefully keeping that answer ambiguous, even from ourselves, for a long time. Because the ambiguity feels safer than the commitment. Because once you've said out loud *this is who I am, this is what I do, this is what I stand for*, you can't un-say it. The video remembers. The lens remembers. The internet remembers.

So the freeze in front of the camera, in my honest assessment after years of watching it happen — is not always about the camera at all. Sometimes it's the body's last defense of a self that hasn't been decided yet. The video can't be recorded because the question underneath the video hasn't been answered.

[MUSIC OUT.]

If that's where you are, the answer isn't more gear. The answer is sitting with the question.

---

## CHAPTER 7 — THE INVITATION + VERSE

[ROOM TONE RETURNS. NO MUSIC.]

So that's the episode.

If you got something from this — if you've been the person sitting in front of a phone you can't bring yourself to press record on — I want to say this with as much care as I can. You are not behind. You are not less brave than the people who post every day. You are not somebody who "isn't a content person." You are a person whose nervous system is doing its job, in a setup that wasn't built for it, while being told that the problem is your discipline.

The problem isn't your discipline. The problem is, mostly, the architecture around you.

That architecture is buildable. Not overnight, and not alone, but buildable. And the work of building it is not glamorous. It's mostly small. Mostly boring. Mostly invisible. Move the desk. Change the light. Set a timer. Do one take a day for thirty days and don't watch any of them until the end of the month. The room gets built quietly, the same way your business got built quietly. One unglamorous decision at a time.

On *MindYourBizniz* we're going to keep coming back to this — the emotional reality of being a business owner who is also a public figure now, whether you wanted that job or not. The cost of visibility. The cost of invisibility. The strange, often unspoken inner life of running something with your name on it.

If that's the kind of conversation you've been needing — stay close. There's more.

[BEAT.]

[VERSE — read slowly, no music underneath.]

> "So God created mankind in his own image."
> — Genesis 1:27

The image. Not the polished one. Not the performed one. Not the one you've been waiting until you're ready to show. The image. The thing that was already there. The thing that's already true.

You don't have to manufacture it. You just have to stop hiding it.

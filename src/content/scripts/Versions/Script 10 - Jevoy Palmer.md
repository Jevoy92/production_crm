# Theme 10: How to Make People Understand You Faster

[VISUAL: Jevoy is standing in a well-lit, slightly messy creative studio. The space feels lived-in—there are cameras on shelves, a whiteboard with half-erased diagrams, and a comfortable leather chair in the corner. He’s holding a small, unlabelled cardboard box. He looks at it, shakes it gently, then looks up at the camera.]

## Chapter 1: The Felt Moment

I found this box in my garage the other day. I was looking for a specific cable—you know the one. It's the one cable you need that you haven't seen in three years, but you know you kept it just in case. You have a drawer full of them, a tangled mess of black wire that belongs to devices you don't even own anymore. But you keep them because the moment you throw them away, you'll need them. That's just how the universe works.

So I'm digging through the garage, moving old paint cans and holiday decorations, and I find this box. No label. No writing. Just a plain, brown cardboard box.

I stared at it for a solid minute. I didn't want to open it, because if I opened it, I'd have to deal with whatever was inside. It could be old tax documents that I need to shred. It could be broken toys that I promised I'd fix but never did. It could be a collection of random screws that belong to furniture I've already thrown away.

But I also couldn't throw it away, because what if it was important? What if it was the cable I was looking for? What if it was my kids' baby teeth? You don't throw away baby teeth, bro. That's bad juju. You keep those in a weird little jar somewhere and hope nobody ever finds them and thinks you're a serial killer.

So the box just sat there. Taking up space. Creating this low-level, humming anxiety in the back of my mind. It was a mystery, but not the fun kind. It was the kind of mystery that just feels like an unresolved chore.

And as I was staring at this unlabelled box, I realized something profound. This is exactly how most people experience your business.

They look at what you do, they look at your website, they look at your social media content, and they see an unlabelled box. They know there's *something* inside. They know it might even be valuable. They can see that you're putting effort into it. But because you haven't told them exactly what it is, they don't know what to do with it.

So they do what I did. They don't throw it away—they might follow you, they might bookmark your page—but they don't open it either. They don't buy. They don't engage. They don't reach out. They just move on to the next thing, because the next thing has a label on it.

I want to investigate this. Because I think we spend so much time trying to get people to look at our box, and almost no time telling them what's inside. We think the problem is attention. We think if we just get more eyeballs, more traffic, more views, then everything will work out. But the problem isn't attention. The problem is translation.

[VISUAL: Jevoy sets the box down on a table. He leans in slightly, his expression shifting from playful to focused.]

How do you make people understand you faster? How do you take the complex, nuanced reality of what you do and turn it into something that someone else can grasp in five seconds? Let's talk about it.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

Here's the mechanism at play. It's called the concreteness effect. It's a psychological principle that says human beings remember and process concrete, specific information much faster and more accurately than abstract, vague information.

Our brains are essentially prediction machines. They are constantly trying to make sense of the world around us, and they want to do it using as little energy as possible. When you give the brain something concrete, it can instantly map it to something it already knows. When you give it something abstract, it has to work overtime to figure out what you mean.

If I say "I have a vehicle," your brain has to do work. Is it a car? A truck? A bicycle? A skateboard? A spaceship? You have to pause and compute. You have to run through a rolodex of possibilities.

If I say "I have a red 1998 Honda Civic with a dent in the left fender," your brain instantly sees it. No computation required. You know exactly what that car looks like. You can probably hear the sound the engine makes. You might even know someone who drove one in high school. The image is immediate and visceral.

When we talk about our businesses, our ideas, or ourselves, we tend to live in the abstract. We say things like, "I provide synergistic solutions for paradigm-shifting enterprises." Or, "I empower visionary leaders to unlock their ultimate potential through holistic transformation."

Bro. Come on now. What does that even mean? You feel me?

We use big, vague words because we think it makes us sound smart. We think it makes us sound professional. We think that if we use the right jargon, people will take us seriously. But all it does is make the other person's brain work harder. And the brain is lazy. If you make it work too hard to understand what you do, it will just stop trying. It will categorize you as "too complicated" and move on.

Specificity reduces misunderstanding. It's that simple. The more specific you are, the less room there is for someone to misinterpret your value.

[VISUAL: Jevoy picks up a marker and walks over to the whiteboard. He writes "SYNERGISTIC SOLUTIONS" in large, block letters. He stares at it for a second, shakes his head, and crosses it out with a thick red line.]

We think we're being profound when we're abstract. But we're actually just being confusing. And confusion is the enemy of connection. You cannot connect with someone who doesn't understand what you're saying. You cannot build trust with someone who is constantly trying to decode your vocabulary.

The ladder of abstraction is a concept developed by a linguist named S.I. Hayakawa back in 1939. He said that language exists on a ladder. At the top of the ladder are abstract concepts—things like "wealth," "success," "freedom." At the bottom of the ladder are concrete things—a hundred dollar bill, a gold medal, a set of car keys.

Most of us try to communicate from the top of the ladder. We talk about our vision, our mission, our core values. But people don't live at the top of the ladder. They live at the bottom. They live in the world of concrete reality. If you want them to understand you, you have to come down the ladder and meet them where they are.

## Chapter 3: The Object

[VISUAL: Jevoy walks back to the table. He holds up two large photographs. One is completely out of focus, just a blur of colors—greens, browns, and a hint of blue. The other is a sharp, high-resolution image of a coffee cup sitting on a wooden table, with steam rising from it.]

Look at these two pictures.

This first one [holds up blurry photo]—this is what most messaging looks like. It's a blur. You can kind of tell there are colors. Maybe it's a landscape? Maybe it's a bowl of fruit? Maybe it's a really abstract painting of a dog? You don't know. So you don't care. Your brain looks at it, realizes it can't make sense of it quickly, and discards it.

This second one [holds up sharp photo]—this is specificity. It's a coffee cup. On a table. You know exactly what it is instantly. You can almost smell the coffee. You know what it feels like to hold that cup.

This is what specificity does. It brings the image into focus.

Vague messaging is like handing someone a map with no street names. You're telling them to go somewhere, but you're not giving them the landmarks they need to navigate. You're saying, "Just drive until you feel like you're in the right place." That's not a strategy. That's a recipe for getting lost.

When you are specific, you are giving people landmarks. You are giving them handles they can grab onto. You are saying, "Here is exactly what this is, and here is exactly how it fits into your life."

[VISUAL: Jevoy puts the photos down and picks up the unlabelled box again. He takes a thick black sharpie and writes "CABLES" in big, bold letters on the side of the box.]

Now I know what's in the box. Now I know what to do with it. If I need a cable, I open it. If I don't need a cable, I put it back on the shelf. But the anxiety is gone. The mystery is solved. The box has a purpose.

Your business needs a label. Your content needs a label. Your ideas need a label. Not a label that puts you in a box you can't escape from, but a label that tells people what they're looking at.

If you are a consultant, don't say you "optimize workflows." Say you "help marketing teams cut their meeting time in half so they can actually get work done."

If you are a designer, don't say you "create compelling visual identities." Say you "design websites that make people want to buy your product the second they land on the page."

Give them the sharp photo. Give them the labelled box. Make it so easy to understand that they couldn't misunderstand it even if they tried.

## Chapter 4: The Counterargument

Now, I know what the skeptic is thinking. I hear you. I've had this conversation a hundred times.

You're sitting there saying, "Jevoy, my business is complex. What I do can't be boiled down to a simple, specific sentence. I have layers. I have nuance. If I get too specific, I'll leave out important details. I'll alienate potential clients who need the other things I do. I don't want to pigeonhole myself."

I respect that. I really do. Complexity is real. Nuance is real. You've spent years building your expertise, and you don't want to dumb down your life's work into a catchy slogan. You want people to appreciate the full depth of what you offer.

But here's the thing: complexity is not an excuse for confusion.

If your idea is complex, your communication has to be even clearer. You don't solve complexity by adding more fog. You solve it by adding more light.

Yes, you might do ten different things. You might have a dozen different services. You might be able to help a hundred different types of people. But if you try to explain all ten things at once, people will remember zero of them. If you try to talk to a hundred different people at once, no one will feel like you're talking to them.

If you explain one specific thing clearly, they will remember you. And once they remember you, and trust you, then you can introduce the other nine things.

Specificity isn't about limiting what you do. It's about creating a clear entry point for people to understand what you do. It's the front door to your house. You don't need to show them the kitchen, the bedrooms, and the basement all at once. You just need to show them the front door and give them a reason to walk inside.

Once they're inside, once they trust you, then you can show them the rest of the house. But if they can't find the front door because it's hidden behind a wall of abstract jargon, they're never going to see the inside anyway.

## Chapter 5: The Flip

[VISUAL: The lighting in the studio shifts slightly, becoming a bit warmer, more intimate. Soft, ambient music begins to play underneath Jevoy's voice—just a subtle pad, nothing distracting.]

And this is where the flip happens. This is the part that most people get wrong.

We think that if we are specific, we will push people away. We think that if we say "I do X for Y," then anyone who isn't Y will leave. We operate from a place of scarcity, terrified of missing out on a single potential opportunity.

But the opposite is true.

Specificity doesn't exclude people; it anchors them.

When you are highly specific, even people who don't fit your exact description will understand your value. Let me give you an example.

If I say, "I build custom wooden dining tables for large families," a single guy living in a one-bedroom apartment might not buy a table from me. He doesn't need a massive dining table. But he knows exactly what I do. He can picture the table. He understands the craftsmanship.

And when his sister, who has four kids and just bought a new house, says she needs a new table, he knows exactly who to recommend. He says, "Oh, you need to talk to Jevoy. He builds these amazing custom wooden dining tables for large families."

Because I gave him a clear, specific picture, I became memorable. I became referable.

When you are vague, nobody knows who to recommend you to. If I say, "I provide woodworking solutions," what does that mean? Do you build houses? Do you carve spoons? Do you fix broken chairs? I don't know. So I don't recommend you. I can't recommend you, because I don't know what problem you actually solve.

Specificity is the fastest path to being understood. And being understood is the fastest path to being trusted.

Trust is the currency of all human interaction. You cannot build a business, you cannot build a brand, you cannot build a relationship without trust. And people do not trust what they do not understand.

When you are specific, you are telling the truth. You are saying, "This is exactly who I am, and this is exactly what I do." There is no hiding. There is no smoke and mirrors. And that kind of honesty builds trust faster than any marketing tactic ever could.

## Chapter 6: The Practical

So how do we actually do this? How do we move from the blurry photo to the sharp one? How do we stop handing out unlabelled boxes and start giving people clear, specific landmarks?

It comes down to asking yourself a few identity questions. And you have to answer them honestly. You can't use jargon. You can't use buzzwords. You have to answer them like you're explaining it to a ten-year-old.

First: What is the actual, tangible problem you solve? Not the philosophical problem. The tangible one. Don't say "I help people find peace." Peace is abstract. Say "I help people organize their finances so they stop fighting with their spouse about money." That is concrete. That is a real problem that real people have.

Second: Who exactly are you solving it for? Not "everyone." If you're for everyone, you're for no one. Pick a person. A specific person with a specific pain point. Don't say "I help entrepreneurs." Say "I help first-time founders who are struggling to hire their first employee." The more specific the person, the more powerful the message.

Third: What is the mechanism? How do you actually do it? Don't say "through my proprietary methodology." That means nothing. Say "through a four-week coaching program where we meet every Tuesday on Zoom." Tell them exactly what the process looks like. Remove the mystery.

[VISUAL: Jevoy is walking slowly across the studio, speaking directly to the camera. The camera tracks him smoothly.]

You have to do the hard work of translation. You have to take the complex, beautiful, messy reality of what you do, and translate it into a language that a stranger can understand in five seconds.

It's not easy. It takes time. It takes iteration. You're going to write it down, and it's going to sound clunky. You're going to try it out on someone, and they're going to look confused. That's okay. That's part of the process.

I'm figuring this out in real time myself. I'm constantly refining how I talk about what I do. I'm constantly trying to find the sharper photo, the clearer label. It's a journey.

But it's the most important work you can do. Because if they don't understand you, they can't choose you. If they don't understand you, they can't trust you. If they don't understand you, you are just another unlabelled box in a world full of noise.

## Chapter 7: Insight, CTA, and Verse

[VISUAL: Jevoy stops walking. He stands still, looking directly into the lens. The music swells slightly, then settles into a quiet, sustained note—a single, resonant tone that holds the space.]

Here is the insight I want to leave you with.

We spend so much time trying to be seen. We want the views, the likes, the attention. We want the algorithm to favor us. We want people to look at our box. We think that visibility is the ultimate goal.

But being seen is not the same as being understood.

You can have a million people look at your unlabelled box, and it won't change your life or your business. It will just give you a fleeting sense of validation that disappears the moment you close the app.

But if you have one hundred people look at a box that is clearly labelled, and it's exactly what they need? That changes everything. That builds a business. That builds a community. That builds a legacy.

Stop trying to be seen by everyone. Start trying to be understood by the right people.

If that question lives inside you too—if you're trying to figure out how to translate your value into something clear and true, if you're tired of being a blurry photo and you want to come into focus—stay close. There's more coming. We're going to figure this out together.

[VISUAL: Jevoy looks down for a moment, letting the silence hang. Then he looks back up. The music fades out completely. He speaks slowly, with quiet, grounded conviction.]

There's a verse in Genesis. Chapter 11, verse 6. It's the story of the Tower of Babel. And it says, "If as one people speaking the same language they have begun to do this, then nothing they plan to do will be impossible for them."

When we speak the same language—when we do the hard work of translation, of making ourselves clear and understood—we build connection. We bridge the gap between my mind and yours.

And when we are connected, when we truly understand each other, there is no limit to what we can build together.

[VISUAL: Jevoy holds the gaze with the camera for a beat. Fade to black.]

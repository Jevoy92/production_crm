# Theme 5: How to Control Your Narrative Before Someone Else Does

[VISUAL: The camera opens on a clean, well-lit studio space. Jevoy Palmer is standing behind a large, sturdy table. He is dressed sharply but comfortably—a tailored jacket over a high-quality t-shirt. The lighting is cinematic, casting soft shadows that give the room depth. On the table sits a large, empty, ornate picture frame, a blank canvas, a whiteboard on an easel to his right, and a thick manila file folder.]

[MUSIC: None. Just the quiet, crisp room tone.]

## Chapter 1: The Founder's Freeze

You know that moment. You’ve been running your business for a few years. You’ve got clients who love you. You’ve got a process that works. You’ve got revenue that proves you’re not just playing around. You are, by all objective measures, a professional. 

But then, someone tells you that you need to "get on video." They tell you that you need to "build a personal brand." They tell you that you need to "control your narrative."

And suddenly, the professional freezes. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy leans slightly forward, resting his hands on the table, looking directly into the lens.]

I’ve seen it happen. I’ve watched brilliant, capable business owners—people who manage payroll, negotiate massive contracts, and solve complex logistical nightmares before breakfast—turn into absolute deer in the headlights the second a lens is pointed at them. 

They freeze. And it’s not because they don’t know what to say. It’s because they know exactly how much is at stake. They know that the moment they speak, they are creating a record. They are putting something out there that can be judged, misunderstood, or taken out of context. 

So, what do they do? They do nothing. They decide that the safest play is to stay quiet. They decide that if they don’t say anything, they can’t say the wrong thing. They believe that silence is a shield. 

But here is the truth, bro. Silence isn’t a shield. Silence is a vacuum. And the market abhors a vacuum. If you don’t tell people who you are, what you do, and why you matter, they aren’t going to wait patiently for you to figure it out. They are going to make it up. They are going to look at your outdated website, your silent social media feeds, and your generic LinkedIn profile, and they are going to write a story about you. 

And I promise you, the story they write will not be as good as the truth. 

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism in Business Terms

Let’s talk about how this actually works in the real world. Let’s talk about the mechanism of perception. 

In psychology, there’s a concept called narrative identity theory. It basically says that we make sense of the world, and of each other, by constructing stories. We don’t just process raw data; we organize that data into a narrative arc. We need a beginning, a middle, and an end. We need heroes, villains, and stakes. 

When a potential client encounters your business for the first time, they are looking for the story. They are looking for the narrative that explains why they should trust you, why they should pay you, and why they should choose you over the ten other people who do exactly what you do. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy picks up the thick manila file folder and drops it onto the table with a heavy thud.]

If you don’t give them the story, they will build one out of whatever scraps they can find. They will look at a single bad review from three years ago. They will look at the fact that your last Instagram post was a blurry picture of a sandwich in 2021. They will look at the fact that your competitor has a slick, professional video explaining their process, while you just have a wall of text on an "About Us" page. 

And they will use those scraps to build a narrative. They will decide that you are outdated. They will decide that you are unprofessional. They will decide that you don’t care enough to show up. 

You cannot control perception. You cannot force someone to see you a certain way. But you can absolutely control the material they use to build their perception. You can provide better evidence. 

Impression management isn’t about lying. It’s not about spin. It’s about making sure that the outside of your business accurately reflects the inside of your business. It’s about making sure that the quality of your communication matches the quality of your work. 

If you are a premium service provider, but your digital presence looks like a discount bin, you are losing money. Not because your work is bad, but because your narrative is broken. 

## Chapter 3: The Empty Frame

[VISUAL: Jevoy picks up the large, empty picture frame. He holds it up so the camera looks right through it at him.]

Look at this frame. It’s empty. There’s nothing in it. 

If I hang this on a wall in a gallery, people are going to walk by and they are going to try to figure out what it means. They’re going to look at the wall behind it. They’re going to look at the lighting. They’re going to project their own ideas onto the empty space. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy lowers the frame and looks intently at the camera.]

This is what happens when you don’t control your narrative. You are handing the market an empty frame. You are saying, "Here, you figure it out. You decide what I’m worth. You decide what I stand for."

Narrative is like wet cement. When you first pour it, you can shape it. You can smooth it out. You can write your name in it. But if you just leave it there, it’s going to set. And whatever falls into it—leaves, dirt, footprints—is going to be locked in permanently. 

If you don’t leave your own imprint early, something else will. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy picks up the blank canvas and places it inside the frame, filling the empty space.]

You have to put something in the frame. You have to provide the picture. You have to tell the market exactly what they are looking at. 

You do this by showing up. You do this by explaining your process. You do this by sharing your philosophy. You do this by letting people hear your voice and see your face. You do this by providing undeniable evidence of your competence. 

When you put the right picture in the frame, you stop the market from guessing. You stop them from making assumptions. You give them the exact narrative you want them to have. 

## Chapter 4: The Strongest Objection

Now, I know what some of you are thinking. I can hear the objection already. 

You’re saying, "Jevoy, this sounds a lot like manipulation. This sounds like you’re telling me to create a fake persona. I don’t want to be one of those slick, overproduced internet marketers who spends more time managing their image than actually doing the work. I want my work to speak for itself."

I hear you. And I respect that. In fact, I agree with the sentiment. The internet is full of people who are all packaging and no product. We’ve all seen the guys standing in front of rented Lamborghinis, selling courses on how to get rich quick. We’ve all seen the businesses that look amazing on Instagram but deliver absolute garbage in reality. 

Nobody wants to be that. I don’t want you to be that. 

But here is where the logic breaks down. You are assuming that controlling your narrative means creating a false narrative. You are assuming that impression management is the same thing as deception. 

It’s not. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy steps out from behind the table and walks over to the whiteboard. He picks up a marker.]

Let me show you the difference. 

[VISUAL: He draws a large circle on the board and writes "THE WORK" inside it. Then he draws a smaller circle outside of it and writes "THE PERCEPTION."]

If your work is incredible, but the perception of your work is terrible, you have a translation problem. You are doing great things, but nobody knows it. 

[VISUAL: He draws an arrow from "THE WORK" to "THE PERCEPTION."]

Controlling your narrative is simply the act of making the perception match the work. It’s not about making yourself look better than you are. It’s about making sure you don’t look worse than you are. 

It’s about translation. It’s about taking the invisible value that exists inside your head, inside your team, and inside your process, and making it visible to the people who need it. 

I’m not talking about spin. I’m talking about evidence. 

## Chapter 5: The Flip

[MUSIC: A low, cinematic, pulsing underscore begins to fade in. It’s serious, driving, but not overwhelming.]

Here is the flip. Here is the moment where the perspective shifts. 

You think that staying quiet is the humble choice. You think that letting your work speak for itself is the honorable path. 

But what if your work can’t speak? What if your work is complex? What if your work requires context to be understood? 

If you have a solution that can genuinely help people—if you have a service that can save a business, or a product that can change a life—and you refuse to explain it clearly because you’re afraid of looking like you’re "managing your image," you aren’t being humble. You’re being selfish. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy walks back to the table. He looks directly into the lens, his voice dropping slightly, becoming more intense.]

You are letting your fear of judgment prevent you from serving the people who need you. 

Controlling your narrative isn’t an ego trip. It’s an act of service. It’s the hard work of making yourself easy to understand. It’s the discipline of removing the friction between your value and the people who need it. 

When you take control of your narrative, you aren’t saying, "Look at how great I am." You are saying, "Here is exactly how I can help you, and here is the proof that I can actually do it."

You are giving them the clarity they need to make a confident decision. 

## Chapter 6: What Palmer House Actually Does

This is exactly why Palmer House Productions exists. 

We are not just a video production company. We are a translation company that happens to own cameras. 

We help businesses become easier to understand, trust, and choose—through strategic cinematic video. We don’t just show up and press record. We don’t just make things look pretty. We build systems of evidence. 

[VISUAL: The camera angle shifts slightly, revealing a glimpse of a light stand and a C-stand in the background—a subtle nod to the production environment.]

We do this through four specific pillars. 

First, the **Spotlight**. This is your brand trust. This is the high-end, cinematic brand film that tells your core story. It’s the video that replaces the empty frame. It’s the anchor that proves you are exactly who you say you are. 

Second, the **Evergreen**. This is your time saved. These are the deep-dive videos that explain your complex processes, answer your most common FAQs, and handle your onboarding. These are the videos that work 24/7, so you don’t have to repeat yourself in every single sales meeting. 

Third, the **Reel**. This is your social reach. We take the deep, strategic content we’ve built and we carve it into sharp, engaging short-form pieces. This isn’t about doing silly dances. This is about putting your actual expertise into the feed, consistently, so you stay top of mind. 

And fourth, the **System**. This is the architecture that holds it all together. We don’t just hand you a hard drive full of files and wish you luck. We build a cohesive ecosystem where every video has a job, every piece of content drives to a goal, and the whole machine is measurable and scalable. 

We take the invisible value of your business and we make it undeniable. We give you the material you need to control your narrative, so the market doesn’t have to guess. 

## Chapter 7: The Invitation

[MUSIC: The pulsing underscore fades out completely. The room tone returns. Quiet. Still.]

[VISUAL: Jevoy stands behind the table. He looks at the empty frame one last time, then looks back to the camera.]

You have built something that matters. You have put in the hours, the sweat, and the capital to create a business that actually solves problems. 

Do not let an empty frame be the reason people walk past you. Do not let silence be the reason they choose a competitor who is half as good but twice as loud. 

Take control of the material. Provide the evidence. Tell the story before someone else makes it up. 

If you are ready to stop guessing and start building a video system that actually translates your value, let’s talk. Book a strategy call with Palmer House Productions. It’s a real conversation, not a sales pitch dressed up as one. We’ll look at your business, we’ll look at the gaps in your narrative, and we’ll figure out exactly what kind of evidence you need to build trust. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy pauses. His expression softens slightly. The business intensity drops, replaced by something deeper, more grounded.]

You know, there’s a reason we care so much about this. There’s a reason we believe that every person, and every honest business, deserves to be seen clearly. 

It goes all the way back to the beginning. Genesis 1:27 says that God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them. 

We are all image-bearers. We all have inherent dignity, inherent value, and a story that deserves to be told with truth and clarity. You don’t have to invent a fake persona to be worthy of attention. You just have to have the courage to show up as you actually are, and the discipline to make that truth visible. 

The frame is waiting. What are you going to put in it? 

[VISUAL: Jevoy holds the camera’s gaze for a long beat. Fade to black.]

# Theme 4: Why Context Doesn't Matter Anymore

## Chapter 1: The Felt Moment

[VISUAL: Close-up on Jevoy's hands. He's holding a physical photograph, but it's folded in half so we only see one side. The visible side shows someone looking angry, pointing a finger. The lighting is moody, cinematic. Jevoy is sitting at a wooden table, maybe a kitchen island or a studio desk. He looks up at the camera.]

I found this picture the other day. Look at this guy. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy taps the folded photo.]

If I asked you to tell me what's happening in this picture, you'd probably say he's yelling at someone. He's aggressive. He's having a terrible day, and he's making sure whoever is on the other side of that finger is having a terrible day too. You feel me? It's obvious. The story writes itself. You look at the tension in the jaw, the angle of the arm, the sheer intensity of the expression, and your brain immediately categorizes it: conflict. Danger. Anger. 

We do this all the time. We are meaning-making machines. We take a fraction of a second of visual data and we construct an entire narrative around it. We decide who the hero is, who the villain is, and what the stakes are, all based on a single, isolated frame.

But watch this.

[VISUAL: Jevoy unfolds the photograph. The full image reveals the man is actually pointing at a scoreboard, celebrating a massive win with his teammates. He's not angry; he's ecstatic. The "aggressive" face is just pure, unfiltered joy.]

Bro. It's a completely different story. The exact same face, the exact same finger, but the meaning completely flips when you see the whole picture. The tension in the jaw isn't anger; it's the release of pressure after a hard-fought victory. The pointed finger isn't an accusation; it's an acknowledgment of the score. The intensity isn't danger; it's triumph.

I want to investigate this. Because we live in a world where everyone is walking around handing out folded photographs of themselves, wondering why people keep getting the story wrong. We take these tiny, cropped moments of our lives, our businesses, our thoughts, and we throw them out into the world. And then we get frustrated when people judge us based on the crop.

We say, "But you don't know the context!" We scream it into the void. We type it furiously in the comments section. We complain about it to our friends. "They took me out of context!" 

Come on now. I'm going to tell you something that might sting a little bit, but it's the truth: Context doesn't matter anymore. Not in the way we think it does. And if you're building anything—a business, a brand, a reputation—relying on people to "understand your context" is the fastest way to lose them. 

We are operating under an outdated assumption. We assume that if we just explain ourselves better, if we just add a longer caption, if we just provide a little more backstory, people will finally get it. But the game has changed. The architecture of how we consume information has fundamentally shifted, and if we don't understand the new rules, we're going to keep getting frustrated by the same old misunderstandings.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

[VISUAL: Jevoy stands up, moving to a different part of the room. Maybe a whiteboard or a blank wall. The camera follows him smoothly. He's energized now, getting into the architecture of the idea.]

Let's break down why this happens. There's a concept in psychology called "context collapse." It was coined by a researcher named danah boyd back in 2008. The idea is simple: in the real world, you act differently depending on who you're with. You have a context. You talk to your mom differently than you talk to your boys. You talk to your clients differently than you talk to your kids. 

In the physical world, context is built into the environment. The room you're in, the clothes you're wearing, the people around you—all of these things provide invisible cues that tell everyone how to interpret your behavior. If you're wearing a suit in a boardroom, your words carry a certain weight. If you're wearing sweatpants in your living room, those same words might be taken as a joke. The environment does the heavy lifting of translation.

But online? In the digital space? All those audiences are collapsed into one room. You're speaking to everyone at the same time, which means you're speaking to no one specifically. Your mom, your boss, your high school nemesis, and a stranger from halfway across the world are all looking at the exact same post, but they are bringing entirely different frameworks to it. 

And here's the kicker: human beings form first impressions in about 100 milliseconds. Willis and Todorov proved this in 2006. One-tenth of a second. That's all it takes for someone to look at your folded photograph and decide exactly who you are. 

Think about how fast you scroll. Think about how quickly your thumb moves across that screen. You are making hundreds of micro-judgments every single minute. You don't have time to investigate the context of every single piece of content you see. Nobody does. 

A clip online is a sentence ripped out of a paragraph and handed to strangers. 

They don't know what you said before. They don't know what you said after. They don't know your heart, your history, or your intentions. They just have the sentence. And the human brain hates a vacuum. If you don't give people the context, they will invent it. They will fill in the blanks with their own assumptions, their own biases, and their own baggage. 

If someone is already having a bad day, they will read your neutral statement as an attack. If someone is already suspicious of your industry, they will read your success story as a scam. They aren't reacting to you; they are reacting to the context they invented to fill the void you left behind.

You can't control perception. But you can provide better material. You can stop handing out folded photographs and start building a gallery where the full picture is undeniable.

## Chapter 3: The Object

[VISUAL: Jevoy walks back to the table. He picks up a book. It's a thick, heavy book. He opens it to a random page right in the middle.]

Look at this book. If I rip this page out...

[VISUAL: Jevoy literally tears a page out of the book. The sound of the paper ripping is loud and crisp.]

...and I hand it to you on the street. You read it. It says something crazy. Something out of pocket. You're going to look at me like I'm out of my mind. 

If I say, "Wait, wait, wait! You have to read the first 200 pages to understand why that character said that! You have to understand the character arc, the thematic development, the historical setting of the novel!" 

Are you going to go buy the book? Are you going to sit down and read 200 pages just to give me the benefit of the doubt? No. You're going to throw the page in the trash and keep walking. You have your own life to live. You have your own problems to solve. You don't owe me your time or your understanding.

That's what we do with content. We throw torn pages at people and demand that they read the whole book. We post a random thought, a fragmented idea, a 15-second clip, and we expect the audience to do the heavy lifting of figuring out who we are and what we stand for. 

We think, "Well, if they just went to my website and read my 'About' page, they would get it." Or, "If they just listened to episode 47 of my podcast, they would understand where I'm coming from." 

But they aren't going to do that. The friction is too high. The attention economy is too ruthless. 

Strategy is not built around what people should do. It's built around what people actually do. And what people actually do is judge the torn page. They look at the 15-second clip, they make a 100-millisecond judgment, and they move on. 

If your entire business model, your entire brand strategy, relies on people doing homework to understand your value, you are going to lose. You are playing a game that no one else is playing. You are asking for an investment of time and attention that you haven't earned yet.

We have to stop blaming the audience for not understanding the context, and start taking responsibility for how we present the information. We have to assume that every piece of content we put out there is a torn page, and we have to design it accordingly.

## Chapter 4: The Counterargument

[VISUAL: Jevoy sits down. The lighting shifts slightly, becoming a bit more intimate. He leans in, acknowledging the pushback.]

Now, I know what the skeptic is saying right now. I hear you. 

You're saying, "Jevoy, that's exactly why I don't post. That's exactly why I stay quiet. Because people are going to misunderstand me anyway. The internet is toxic. People take things out of context on purpose just to be mad. It's a blood sport out there. So the safest play is to just keep my head down, do my work, and let my real-life reputation speak for itself. I don't need to play this game."

I respect that. I really do. There is a dignity in quiet work. There is a wisdom in not needing to broadcast every thought that crosses your mind. We don't all need to be influencers. We don't all need to have a hot take on every single issue. And you're right—people will misunderstand you. That is a guarantee. No matter how clear you are, someone will find a way to twist your words.

But let's play that out. Let's say you stay completely quiet. You never share your process. You never explain your value. You never let people see how you think. You rely entirely on word-of-mouth and the people who already know you.

What happens then? What happens when someone who doesn't know you tries to find out who you are?

Silence is not neutral. Silence is an empty billboard. You might think it says nothing, but people still read into the blank space. If you don't tell people who you are, they will decide who you are based on the absolute bare minimum of information. 

They will look at your outdated LinkedIn profile. They will look at a random review from three years ago. They will look at the fact that you have no digital footprint and they will assume that you are either irrelevant, out of touch, or hiding something. 

You think you're protecting yourself by staying quiet, but you're actually just handing the pen to strangers and letting them write your story. You are abdicating responsibility for your own narrative. 

The choice isn't between being perfectly understood and being misunderstood. The choice is between actively shaping the narrative or letting the void shape it for you. If you don't provide the evidence of your competence, your character, and your value, the market will simply assume it doesn't exist.

## Chapter 5: The Flip

[VISUAL: The music finally drops in. It's not aggressive, but it has a pulse. A steady, driving rhythm that signals a shift in perspective. Jevoy looks directly into the lens. The camera slowly pushes in.]

Here is the flip. 

The problem isn't that context doesn't matter. The problem is that we expect context to travel *with* the content. It doesn't. 

Context is not something you can attach to a single post or a single video. Context is the room you build around the content. 

Think about it. If you walk into a beautiful, high-end restaurant, and the waiter drops a plate, you don't think, "This place is garbage." You think, "Oh, an accident happened." Why? Because the context—the lighting, the decor, the greeting at the door, the menu, the smell of the food, the ambient music—has already established trust. The room holds the mistake. The architecture of the experience is so strong that a single error doesn't destroy the entire perception.

But if you walk into a dirty, run-down diner, the floor is sticky, the lights are flickering, and the waiter drops a plate, you think, "I need to leave right now. This place is a disaster." 

The action was the exact same. The dropped plate is the torn page. But the context changed the meaning entirely. 

You don't build context by explaining yourself in the comments. You don't build context by adding a disclaimer to every single thing you say. You build context by building a body of work. You build it by showing up consistently, with intention, over a long period of time. 

You build it so that when someone finally sees that one torn page, they already know what book it came from. They might see a 15-second clip of you saying something provocative, but because they've seen your long-form essays, because they've listened to your podcast, because they've watched your detailed breakdowns, they know your heart. They know your methodology. They give you the benefit of the doubt because you have earned it.

Context is not a caption. Context is a reputation built on a foundation of consistent, high-quality evidence.

## Chapter 6: The Practical

[VISUAL: Jevoy is standing again, maybe in a different part of the room with better, brighter lighting. He's in architect mode now. Practical, actionable.]

So how do we actually do this? How do we build the room? 

I'm figuring this out in real time myself. I'm launching these ventures, I'm putting my own work out there, and I'm realizing that I can't just throw things at the wall. I have to build the architecture. I have to construct the room before I invite people inside.

Here are three principles I'm using to build context before the market creates assumptions.

First: Stop defending the crop. When someone misunderstands a single piece of content, your instinct is to fight them in the comments. To explain the context. To write a five-paragraph essay defending your honor. Don't waste your energy. The crop is already the crop. The 100-millisecond judgment has already been made. Instead of defending the folded photograph, just keep publishing the full picture. Let your next piece of work be the answer. Let your consistency be your defense.

Second: Build anchors, not just fireworks. Fireworks get attention. They go viral, they make a lot of noise, they light up the sky for a few seconds, and then they disappear. We are obsessed with fireworks. But anchors are the foundational pieces of content that explain who you are, what you believe, and how you work. You need anchors. You need long-form videos, detailed essays, or comprehensive podcasts that people can find when they want to go deeper. The fireworks might bring them in, but the anchors give them the context. If all you have is fireworks, you just have a lot of noise and no substance.

Third: Own your framing. If you don't put anything in the frame, people will. You have to be proactive about telling your story. What are your core values? What is your methodology? Why do you do what you do? Say it clearly, say it often, and say it before anyone asks. Don't wait for a crisis to explain your principles. Establish your principles so clearly that when a crisis hits, your audience already knows where you stand.

You are not responsible for how people interpret your work. You cannot control the cognitive biases of strangers on the internet. But you are absolutely responsible for the quality of the evidence you leave behind.

## Chapter 7: The Insight

[VISUAL: The music fades out completely. Silence. Jevoy is sitting back at the table. The lighting is warm, focused. He speaks slower now, letting the words land.]

We spend so much time worrying about being taken out of context. We let the fear of misunderstanding paralyze us. We fold the photograph so small that eventually, we're just hiding. We dilute our message, we soften our edges, and we try to be everything to everyone, which means we end up meaning nothing to anyone.

But the goal isn't to be perfectly understood by everyone. That's impossible. The goal is to be accurately understood by the right people. 

And that requires you to show up. It requires you to unfold the picture. It requires you to build the room, brick by brick, video by video, story by story, until the context is so undeniable that a single torn page could never define you. It requires the courage to be seen, even when you know that some people will get it wrong.

If that question lives inside you too—if you're trying to figure out how to build that room without losing your mind, if you're trying to figure out how to show up authentically in a world that loves to crop the picture—stay close. There's more. We're going to figure this out together.

[VISUAL: Jevoy looks down at the table. A beat of silence. He takes a slow breath.]

Genesis 1:27 says, "So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."

We are all carrying an image. We are all reflecting something much bigger than a single moment, a single mistake, or a single cropped photograph. You have inherent dignity, not because of how perfectly you present yourself, not because of how well you manage your personal brand, but because of whose image you bear. 

Don't let a torn page convince you otherwise. Don't let the fear of being misunderstood keep you from sharing the full picture.

[VISUAL: Fade to black.]

# How to Make People Understand You Faster

[VISUAL: Wide shot of a modern, slightly industrial studio space. The lighting is moody but clean. Jevoy Palmer is sitting on a stool, leaning forward, looking directly into the lens. No music. Just the ambient room tone.]

## Chapter 1: The Founder's Freeze

[VISUAL: Cut to a tighter shot. Jevoy holds a smartphone in his hand, screen dark. He taps it against his palm rhythmically.]

I want to talk about a moment that costs businesses more money than bad marketing, more money than economic downturns, and more money than poor product design. It is a quiet moment. It happens in home offices, in parked cars before walking into a networking event, and right in front of a camera lens. 

I call it the founder's freeze. 

You know exactly what I am talking about. You sit down to record a video for your business. You have been doing this work for years. You know your industry inside and out. You know the exact pain points your clients have, and you know exactly how to solve them. You are an absolute expert in your field. But the second you hit record, or the second someone asks you, "So, what exactly do you do?"... you freeze. 

Or worse, you don't freeze. You start talking, and you talk for five minutes straight, using industry jargon, high-level concepts, and abstract ideas. You watch the other person's eyes glaze over. You watch them nod politely, but you know—you absolutely know—they have no idea what you just said. 

Bro, I see it all the time. I have sat across from brilliant business owners, people who are changing lives with their services, and watched them struggle to articulate their value in a way that a normal human being can understand. 

This is not a lack of knowledge. It is a translation problem. You are speaking a language that your prospect does not speak. You are handing them a dictionary when they just need directions. 

And here is the brutal truth about the founder's freeze: confusion is the enemy of conversion. If people do not understand you quickly, they do not stick around to figure it out. They do not ask for clarification. They just leave. They go to the competitor who might not be as good as you, but who is easier to understand. 

You feel me? It is incredibly frustrating to lose to someone who is less qualified, simply because they were more clear. 

We live in an attention economy, but that is only half the story. We actually live in a comprehension economy. Attention gets them to look at you. Comprehension gets them to trust you. If you cannot make people understand you faster, you are leaving massive amounts of opportunity on the table. You are making your business harder to choose. 

So, how do we fix this? How do we take all that complex expertise in your head and turn it into something that people instantly grasp? 

## Chapter 2: Mechanism in Business Terms

[VISUAL: Jevoy stands up and walks slowly toward a large whiteboard or a clean, blank wall. The camera tracks him smoothly.]

To understand why we struggle to explain our own businesses, we have to look at the psychology of communication. There is a reason you sound confusing when you try to explain what you do, and it is not because you are not smart. It is actually because you are too close to the work. 

Back in 1939, a linguist named S.I. Hayakawa introduced a concept called the "ladder of abstraction." Imagine a ladder. At the very top of the ladder are highly abstract concepts: "synergy," "optimization," "holistic solutions," "strategic alignment." These are words that mean everything and nothing at the same time. At the bottom of the ladder are concrete, tangible things: a red apple, a signed contract, a broken pipe, a $500 invoice. 

When you are an expert, you live at the top of the ladder. You think in systems and abstract concepts because you have already mastered the concrete details. But your client? Your client is standing at the bottom of the ladder. They are dealing with a specific, concrete problem. They have a broken pipe. They have a team that will not communicate. They have a tax bill they cannot pay. 

When you speak from the top of the ladder to someone at the bottom, the message gets lost in the air. 

There is also something in psychology called the concreteness effect, heavily researched by Allan Paivio in 1971 through his dual coding theory. Paivio found that human beings process and remember concrete information significantly better than abstract information. Why? Because concrete words activate both the verbal and the visual parts of our brain. If I say "apple," you hear the word and you see the fruit. If I say "optimization," you hear the word, but you see nothing. There is no image attached to it. 

In business terms, this means that if your marketing, your videos, and your sales pitches are full of abstract concepts, your audience's brain is working overtime just to figure out what you are talking about. And the human brain hates burning unnecessary calories. If it takes too much effort to understand your value proposition, the brain will simply tune out. 

This is what vague messaging costs your business. It creates friction. It creates a cognitive load that your prospect did not ask for and will not tolerate. 

You have to climb down the ladder. You have to stop talking about "holistic financial empowerment" and start talking about "keeping more of the money you make." You have to stop talking about "synergistic operational efficiencies" and start talking about "getting your team to stop missing deadlines." 

Specificity reduces misunderstanding. It is the fastest way to transfer an idea from your brain into someone else's brain without losing the meaning in transit. 

## Chapter 3: The Object

[VISUAL: Jevoy walks over to a sleek, industrial table. On the table are three items: a blurry photograph in a frame, a sharply focused photograph in an identical frame, and a map with no text on it.]

Let me show you exactly what I mean. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy picks up the blurry photograph. It is an image of a landscape, but it is completely out of focus. Just smudges of green and blue.]

Look at this. If I hand this to you and ask, "What is this a picture of?" you might guess. You might say it is a forest, or a park, or maybe a blurry green car. You have to work to figure it out. You have to squint. You have to ask questions. 

This is what your business looks like to a prospect when you use vague, abstract language. It is a blurry picture. They know you do *something* in the realm of consulting, or coaching, or software, but they cannot see the sharp edges of your value. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy sets the blurry photo down and picks up the sharply focused photo. It is a crystal-clear image of a specific pine tree on a rocky cliff.]

Now look at this. Instant recognition. Zero cognitive load. You know exactly what it is the second you look at it. 

This is what specificity does. It brings your business into sharp focus. It removes the guesswork. 

[VISUAL: Jevoy sets the photo down and picks up the map. He unfolds it. It shows roads, rivers, and borders, but there are absolutely no names, no labels, no legend.]

Or think about it like this. Vague messaging is like handing someone a map with no street names. 

Imagine you are lost in a new city. You ask me for directions, and I hand you this map. It has all the roads drawn out perfectly. The topography is accurate. But there are no labels. No "Main Street." No "Highway 99." No "You Are Here" star. 

Is this map accurate? Yes. Is it helpful? Absolutely not. 

When you talk about your business in broad, sweeping terms, you are handing your clients an unlabeled map. You are giving them accurate information, but you are not giving them the specific markers they need to navigate their way to a decision. 

You have to give them the street names. You have to give them the legend. You have to label the box. 

If you want people to understand you faster, you have to stop making them squint at blurry pictures and stop making them navigate unlabeled maps. You have to do the hard work of translating your complex expertise into concrete, specific, undeniable clarity. 

## Chapter 4: The Strongest Objection

[VISUAL: Jevoy leans against the table, crossing his arms. The camera angle shifts slightly, adopting a more confrontational, yet respectful, perspective.]

Now, I know exactly what some of you are thinking right now. I hear this objection all the time, and it is the strongest objection there is. 

You are sitting there thinking, "Jevoy, that sounds great for a simple product. But my business is nuanced. What I do is highly complex. I deal with intricate legal frameworks, or advanced software architecture, or deep psychological trauma. I cannot just dumb it down to a picture of a pine tree. If I oversimplify it, I lose the essence of what makes my service valuable."

I hear you. Come on now, you feel me? I respect that. Your work *is* complex. The problems you solve are not simple, and the solutions you provide require deep expertise. 

But here is where we have to draw a hard line: complexity is not an excuse for confusion. 

Let me say that again, because it is the trap that catches so many brilliant founders. Complexity is not an excuse for confusion. 

If the idea is complex, the communication has to be even clearer. 

There is a massive difference between dumbing something down and translating it. Dumbing it down means removing the value, removing the nuance, and treating your audience like they are stupid. Translating it means taking the complex value and packaging it in a way that a non-expert can actually carry. 

Think about a doctor. A brilliant surgeon understands the human body at a level of complexity that would make my head spin. But when that surgeon comes into the waiting room to talk to the family, they do not read from a medical textbook. They do not use Latin terms for every blood vessel. They use analogies. They use concrete examples. They translate the complexity into clarity. 

Does that mean the surgery was simple? No. It means the communication was effective. 

If you are hiding behind the complexity of your business as a reason for why your marketing is confusing, you are doing a disservice to the people who need your help. You are making them work too hard to figure out if you can actually solve their problem. 

You do not need to dumb down your expertise. You need to sharpen your translation. 

## Chapter 5: The Flip

[VISUAL: The lighting shifts subtly, becoming slightly warmer. A low, cinematic, pulsing score begins to fade in. It is not overwhelming, just a steady heartbeat of sound that signals a shift in perspective.]

This changes everything when you finally see it. 

The moment you realize that clarity is not a marketing tactic, but an act of service to your client, your entire approach to video and communication flips. 

You stop trying to sound smart, and you start trying to be understood. 

You realize that the most powerful businesses in the world are not the ones with the most complex explanations. They are the ones that can take a massive, complicated problem and articulate it so clearly that the client says, "Yes. That is exactly what I am experiencing. How did you know?"

When you make people understand you faster, you accelerate trust. Trust is the currency of business. People do not buy what they do not understand, and they certainly do not trust what confuses them. 

This is the flip. You have to stop viewing your content, your videos, and your messaging as a place to prove how much you know. You have to start viewing it as a bridge. 

Your expertise is on one side of the river. Your client's problem is on the other side. Vague, abstract language is a bridge made of fog. It looks impressive from a distance, but the second they step on it, they fall through. 

Specific, concrete, clear communication is a bridge made of steel. It holds their weight. It gets them from their problem to your solution safely. 

And here is the beautiful part: when you build that bridge of clarity, you stop having to convince people to work with you. The clarity does the convincing. When they understand exactly what you do, exactly who it is for, and exactly how it solves their specific problem, the decision becomes obvious. 

You become easier to understand. You become easier to trust. And you become infinitely easier to choose.

- **Chapter 2:** Smooth tracking shot as Jevoy moves to the whiteboard/wall. This physical movement mirrors the intellectual movement of explaining the psychology (ladder of abstraction).

# YourBoyJevoy — "Something on My Mind" Scripts
## 10 Ready-to-Record Shorts (60-90 seconds each)

*Format rules: One observation. One gap. One honest landing. Opener is always "Something on my mind is…" or a variation. End when the thought is complete. No formal close needed.*

---

## SCRIPT 1: Sheltered Kids Get Sneaky Kids

Something on my mind is how sheltering kids doesn't actually protect them.

Like — I grew up in the 90s. And parents were genuinely trying. The intention was love. "If I keep my kid away from things, I'm keeping them safe." And I get it. I really do.

But the outcome? Was just secrecy.

Because kids still got into stuff. They just did it behind your back. And then the reward for all that protection was — your kid didn't come to you when something real happened. Because their whole system was trained: "You can't bring that here."

And that's how people end up carrying things for ten years before they tell anyone. Not because they're bad. Because they learned early that silence is safer than honesty.

I'm not raising sheltered kids. I'm raising prepared kids. Exposure with conversation. Awareness with values.

Because God isn't scared of truth. And I don't want my children to be either.

---

## SCRIPT 2: We Call Avoidance "Peace"

Something on my mind is how we've confused avoiding conflict with actually having peace.

Like — we walk around calling it "keeping the peace." But what we're actually doing is keeping the silence. And silence isn't peace. It's just quiet tension with better manners.

Real peace is what happens after the hard conversation. After someone says the thing nobody wanted to say, and both people are still standing, and the relationship is actually stronger because of it.

But we're so allergic to discomfort that we'll choose the quiet tension every time. And then wonder why nothing ever really changes.

The room doesn't need to be comfortable. Sometimes the room needs to be awkward — because the truth is trying to get in, and we keep locking the door.

I'm trying to be the person who opens the door. Even when it's uncomfortable. Especially when it's uncomfortable.

---

## SCRIPT 3: Confidence Isn't Wisdom

Something on my mind is how confidence and wisdom are not the same thing — and we keep treating them like they are.

Like — confident people get listened to. That's just how it works. They speak with certainty, they take up space, they don't second-guess themselves out loud. And we read that as competence.

But confidence is just a feeling about yourself. Wisdom is actually knowing what you're talking about.

And the scary part? The most confident people in the room are sometimes the least equipped to be making the decisions they're making. They just don't know what they don't know. And they're comfortable enough with themselves that the not-knowing doesn't bother them.

My confidence isn't wisdom. I have to remind myself of that. Because I can feel certain about something and still be wrong. And the certainty doesn't make me right — it just makes me loud.

That's worth sitting with.

---

## SCRIPT 4: Hard Conversations Are Love

Something on my mind is how we've decided that love means making people comfortable.

Like — if you love someone, you don't say the hard thing. You protect them. You let it go. You keep the peace.

But I've been thinking about that. And I don't think that's love. I think that's comfort. And comfort and love are not the same thing.

Love is the person who tells you the thing you needed to hear even though it was going to make the conversation awkward. Love is the friend who says "bro, that's not okay" instead of "yeah, you're right, they're terrible."

Comfort protects the relationship from tension. Love protects the person from themselves.

And I'd rather be loved than comfortable. Even when it doesn't feel like it in the moment.

---

## SCRIPT 5: The Christian Whisper Problem

Something on my mind is how Christians will whisper about the exact things the church should be talking about loudly.

Like — OnlyFans. Masturbation. Addiction. Mental health. Abuse. These aren't fringe topics. These are things happening in every congregation, every family, every pew. And we talk about them in hushed tones, in back rooms, in "prayer requests" that are really just coded confessions.

And the message that sends — especially to young people — is: "You can't bring that here." Which means when something real happens, they don't come to us. They go somewhere else. Or they go nowhere. And they carry it alone.

I'm not saying the church needs to be reckless. I'm saying the silence is doing more damage than the honesty would.

Light doesn't make things worse. It just makes them visible. And visible is where healing starts.

---

## SCRIPT 6: My Kid Isn't My Best Friend

Something on my mind is how "my kid is my best friend" became a parenting flex — and I'm not sure it should be.

Like — I love my kids. Deeply. But I'm not trying to be their best friend. I'm trying to be their father. And those are different jobs.

A best friend validates you. A parent challenges you. A best friend lets you make the decision. A parent makes the decision for you sometimes — even when you hate it — because they can see further down the road than you can.

And when we collapse that distinction, we end up with kids who don't have guidance because their parent is too busy being liked. And we end up with parents who can't make hard calls because they're afraid of losing the friendship.

I want my kids to like me. But I need them to trust me more than they like me. And sometimes those two things are in conflict.

That's okay. That's the job.

---

## SCRIPT 7: We Perform for the People Who Already Know Us

Something on my mind is how we're often more honest with strangers than with the people closest to us.

Like — think about it. You'll tell a stranger on a plane something you've never told your family. You'll be more yourself at a networking event than at Christmas dinner. You'll say the real thing in a first conversation that you've been avoiding in a ten-year relationship.

And I think it's because the stakes are different. With strangers, there's no history to protect. No version of you that needs to be maintained. No relationship that could be damaged by the truth.

But with the people who know you — there's a whole performance running. The version of you they expect. The version of you you've always been in that relationship. And stepping outside that version feels risky.

So we perform for the people who already know us. And we're real with the people who don't.

That's backwards. And I'm trying to flip it.

---

## SCRIPT 8: Shame Is the Real Drug

Something on my mind is how shame doesn't stop people from doing things — it just makes them do those things in secret.

Like — we use shame as a behavior modification tool. "You should be ashamed of yourself." And the idea is that the shame will make the person stop. But that's not what actually happens.

What actually happens is the person keeps doing the thing — they just stop telling anyone about it. The shame doesn't change the behavior. It just drives it underground.

And underground is where things get worse. Because now there's no accountability, no community, no one to help. Just the behavior and the shame and the silence.

I'm not saying there's no place for conviction. There is. But conviction says "this isn't who you want to be." Shame says "this is who you are." And those land completely differently.

One opens a door. The other locks it.

---

## SCRIPT 9: The Gap Between Who We Say We Are and Who We Actually Are

Something on my mind is how the gap between who we say we are and who we actually are is where most of our problems live.

Like — we all have a stated version of ourselves. The values we claim. The person we describe when someone asks. "I'm patient." "I'm honest." "I'm a good listener." "I put family first."

And then there's the actual version. The one that shows up under pressure. The one that comes out when we're tired or scared or threatened. The one our kids see. The one our spouse sees. The one that appears when nobody's watching.

And the gap between those two versions — that's not a character flaw. That's just human. We all have it.

The question is whether we're honest about it. Whether we're working on it. Or whether we're just maintaining the stated version for the audience and hoping nobody looks too closely at the actual version.

I'm trying to close the gap. Slowly. Imperfectly. But intentionally.

---

## SCRIPT 10: Walking on Eggshells Is a Choice

Something on my mind is how we act like walking on eggshells is something that happens to us — when really it's something we choose.

Like — nobody forces you to avoid the topic. Nobody makes you change the subject. Nobody requires you to give the vague answer instead of the honest one.

We choose it. Because the alternative is uncomfortable. Because the conversation might go somewhere we don't want it to go. Because the other person might react in a way we're not prepared for.

And I get it. I've done it. I still do it sometimes.

But I've been thinking about what it costs. Because every time you walk on eggshells, you're telling the other person — without saying it — that their reaction matters more than the truth. That keeping them comfortable is more important than being real with them.

And that's not respect. That's management.

I'd rather be respected than managed. And I'm trying to offer the same thing to the people I love.

# SCRIPT 5: "How to Control Your Narrative Before Someone Else Does"

## CHAPTER 1: THE VACUUM PROBLEM

Here's something I've been thinking about. When there's no information about you, people don't think nothing. They think something. They fill the gap. And what they fill it with isn't neutral.

The human brain doesn't tolerate informational vacuums. When it encounters a person it doesn't have information about, it doesn't suspend judgment. It constructs one. It pulls from whatever's available — appearance, context, associations, assumptions — and builds a picture. That picture may have nothing to do with who you actually are. But it feels real to the person who built it. And it shapes how they interact with you from that point forward.

This is the narrative vacuum problem. And it's not abstract. It happens every time someone Googles you and finds nothing. Every time someone asks about you and nobody has anything to say. Every time a potential client, collaborator, or employer tries to form an impression of you and has no material to work with.

The vacuum doesn't stay empty. It gets filled. The question is whether you're involved in what fills it.

I want to investigate how narrative actually forms — not in theory, but in practice. How people construct impressions of others. What happens when those impressions form without input from the person being evaluated. And what it actually means to control your narrative before someone else does.

## CHAPTER 2: HOW REPUTATION FORMS WITHOUT YOU

There's a concept in social psychology called the fundamental attribution error. It describes the tendency to explain other people's behavior through character rather than circumstance. When you do something, you know all the context. When someone else does something, you see the behavior and you attribute it to who they are.

This is the engine of reputation formation. People observe behavior — or hear about behavior, or see a clip of behavior — and they draw conclusions about character. Not about circumstances. About character. About who the person fundamentally is.

And here's what's important to understand. This process doesn't require much input. A few observations, a few secondhand accounts, a few data points — and the brain has enough to construct a working model of who you are. That model may be incomplete. It may be inaccurate. But it's functional. It's the model the person will use to interpret everything else they encounter about you.

Now here's the part that matters for narrative control. The model that forms in the absence of your input is built from whatever's available. If you have a presence — content you've created, things you've said publicly, work you've done that's visible — the model gets built from that. If you don't, it gets built from secondhand accounts, from associations, from assumptions based on limited information.

The secondhand model is almost always less accurate than the firsthand one. Not because people are trying to misrepresent you. But because they're working with incomplete information. They're filling gaps with assumptions. And the assumptions are shaped by their own biases, their own experiences, their own frame of reference — not yours.

This is why the absence of a narrative isn't neutral. It's an invitation for other people to construct one. And the one they construct is built to make sense to them, not to represent you accurately.

## CHAPTER 3: THE MECHANISM OF NARRATIVE CONTROL

Here's what narrative control actually is. Not spin. Not PR. Not carefully managing what people see. Narrative control is the practice of providing enough accurate information about yourself that the model people build of you is built from real material.

The brain is going to build a model regardless. The question is what it's building from. If you've given it accurate, representative, consistent information — through content you've created, through work you've done publicly, through the way you show up consistently over time — then the model it builds is more likely to be accurate.

If you haven't, the model gets built from whatever's available. And whatever's available is usually incomplete, secondhand, and filtered through other people's interpretations.

Narrative control isn't about controlling what people think. You can't do that. It's about ensuring that what people think is based on real information rather than constructed assumptions.

And here's the mechanism that makes this work. When you provide consistent, accurate information about yourself over time, you create what psychologists call a schema — a mental framework that people use to organize and interpret information about you. Once a schema is established, new information gets interpreted through it. Behavior that fits the schema gets noted and remembered. Behavior that contradicts it gets treated as an anomaly.

This is why consistency matters more than volume. A hundred inconsistent pieces of content don't build a schema. They create confusion. Ten consistent pieces of content that clearly communicate who you are and what you're about — those build a schema. And once the schema is built, it does the interpretive work for you.

## CHAPTER 4: THE COMPLICATION

Here's where this gets complicated. Most people, when they think about controlling their narrative, think about it defensively. They think about what they want to prevent people from thinking. What they want to correct. What they want to manage.

That's the wrong frame. Defensive narrative control is reactive. It's trying to undo impressions that have already formed. And as we've established, impressions that have already formed are very hard to undo. The anchor is set. The schema is built. New information gets filtered through it.

Proactive narrative control is different. It's about building the schema before the impression forms. It's about providing enough accurate information that when people encounter you — or encounter information about you — they already have a frame for interpreting it.

The complication is that proactive narrative control requires you to be visible before you feel ready. Before you have everything figured out. Before you know exactly what you want to say. And most people aren't comfortable with that. They want to wait until they have the perfect thing to say. Until they've built something impressive enough to share. Until they feel ready.

But here's the thing. The narrative vacuum doesn't wait for you to be ready. It gets filled in the meantime. And what fills it is whatever's available — which is often not what you would choose.

The discomfort of showing up before you feel ready is real. But it's smaller than the cost of letting the vacuum fill without you.

## CHAPTER 5: THE CONSEQUENCE OF SILENCE

Here's what actually happens when you don't control your narrative.

It's not that nothing happens. Something always happens. The vacuum fills. The question is with what.

In professional contexts, the absence of a visible presence often gets interpreted as a lack of credibility. Not because people are being unfair. But because credibility, in the modern context, is partly demonstrated through visibility. If you can't find evidence of someone's work, their thinking, their approach — if they're essentially invisible — the default assumption is that there isn't much to find.

This is a relatively new dynamic. For most of human history, reputation was built locally. Through direct relationships. Through word of mouth in bounded communities. You didn't need a public presence to have a good reputation. Your reputation existed in the community you were part of.

Now, the community is global and the evaluation is often done by people who have no relationship with you. They're evaluating you based on what they can find. And if they can't find much, the evaluation defaults to skepticism.

This isn't fair. But it's real. And understanding that it's real is the first step to doing something about it.

The consequence of silence isn't neutrality. It's a default negative. Not a strong negative. Not a damning judgment. Just a slight tilt toward skepticism. A slight reduction in the benefit of the doubt. A slight increase in the friction between you and the opportunities you're trying to create.

Multiply that slight tilt across every interaction where someone evaluates you without knowing you, and the cumulative effect is significant.

## CHAPTER 6: WHAT NARRATIVE CONTROL ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE

Here's the practical version of this. Narrative control isn't a campaign. It's not a strategy you execute once. It's a practice. It's the ongoing work of showing up consistently in ways that accurately represent who you are.

It looks like creating content that demonstrates your thinking. Not just your conclusions — your thinking. The way you approach problems. The questions you ask. The things you find interesting. The way you work through ideas. That's what builds a schema. Not polished outputs. Process.

It looks like being specific about what you do and why. Not in a marketing way. In a genuine way. The clearest signal of who you are isn't your bio. It's the specificity of what you talk about and how you talk about it. Specificity is credibility. Vagueness is the enemy of narrative control.

It looks like consistency over time. Not posting every day. But showing up regularly enough that there's a pattern. A body of work. A record. Something that gives people material to build an accurate model from.

And it looks like being honest about where you are. Not performing a version of yourself that's further along than you actually are. Not claiming authority you haven't earned. Just showing up as you actually are, doing the work you're actually doing, thinking about the things you're actually thinking about.

That honesty is what makes the narrative sustainable. A performed narrative eventually collapses. An honest one compounds.

## CHAPTER 7: THE INSIGHT

Here's the uncomfortable truth about narrative control. You can't fully control your narrative. You can influence it. You can provide material for it. You can build a foundation that makes it harder to misrepresent you. But you can't control what people think.

What you can control is whether the impressions people form are based on real information or constructed assumptions. Whether the model they build of you is built from material you've provided or from gaps they've filled themselves.

That's the actual choice. Not between being understood and being misunderstood. Between being misunderstood based on real information or being misunderstood based on no information.

Real information at least gives you a chance. Absence gives you nothing to work with.

The people who navigate this best aren't the ones who've figured out how to be perfectly understood. They're the ones who've built enough of a presence that misunderstanding is the exception rather than the default. Because the material is there. The pattern is clear. The schema is built.

That's the goal. Not perfect narrative control. Enough presence that the vacuum doesn't define you.

Proverbs 27:2 says: "Let someone else praise you, and not your own mouth." But that assumes someone else knows enough about you to praise you accurately. The work of narrative control is making sure they do. Not by promoting yourself. By showing up consistently enough that the people who encounter your work have something real to say about it.

## EXTENDED REFLECTION: THE LONG GAME

I want to add something here because I think it's easy to hear "control your narrative" and think it means being strategic in a calculating way. Managing perceptions. Crafting an image. Presenting a version of yourself that's designed to land well.

That's not what I'm talking about. And I think that version of narrative control is actually counterproductive.

Here's why. When you're performing a narrative — when you're presenting a version of yourself that's designed rather than genuine — the performance is detectable. Not always consciously. But people sense it. There's a quality to performed content that's different from genuine content. A kind of smoothness that reads as polish but feels like distance. A kind of consistency that reads as brand but feels like script.

Genuine narrative control is different. It's not about presenting the best version of yourself. It's about consistently showing up as the actual version of yourself. Thinking out loud. Working through ideas in public. Sharing the process, not just the output. Being honest about uncertainty, not just confident about conclusions.

That kind of presence is harder to fake. And it's much more durable. Because it's not a performance that has to be maintained. It's just you, showing up consistently, doing the work you're actually doing.

The paradox of narrative control is that the most effective version of it is the one that feels least like control. Not because you're not being intentional. But because the intention is to be genuinely yourself, consistently, over time. And genuine consistency is its own kind of control. It builds a pattern that's recognizable. A presence that's real. A reputation that's earned rather than constructed.

That's the long game. Not managing perceptions. Building a presence that accurately represents you, consistently, over time. Until the gap between who you are and how you're perceived is as small as it can be.

You won't close it completely. Nobody does. But you can make it small enough that it doesn't cost you the things that matter.

That's the work. And it starts with showing up. Not perfectly. Not with everything figured out. Just consistently. Just honestly. Just as yourself.

Because the alternative — letting the vacuum fill without you — is always more expensive than the discomfort of showing up before you feel ready.

## THE PRACTICAL QUESTION

Here's the question I get asked most often when this topic comes up. "Where do I start?"

And the honest answer is: with the thing you're already thinking about. Not with the thing you think you should be talking about. Not with the topic that seems most strategic. With the actual question that's occupying your mind right now.

Because here's what I've noticed. The content that builds the most durable presence isn't the content that's most polished or most strategic. It's the content that's most genuine. The content that comes from a real place of curiosity or conviction or frustration or discovery.

When you're genuinely interested in something, it shows. The specificity is different. The energy is different. The thinking is different. And people can feel the difference between someone who's talking about something because they find it genuinely interesting and someone who's talking about something because they think they should.

The first kind of content builds a real presence. The second kind builds a performed one. And performed presences are fragile. They require constant maintenance. They collapse under pressure. They don't compound over time the way genuine ones do.

So the starting point for narrative control isn't a strategy session. It's an honest inventory. What do you actually care about? What questions are you actually trying to answer? What work are you actually doing? What do you actually know that other people don't?

Start there. Not with the audience. Not with the algorithm. Not with what you think will perform well. With what's actually true about you and your work.

That's the foundation of a narrative that can last. Not a brand. Not a strategy. Just consistent, honest presence over time. Building the body of work that gives people real material to build an accurate model from.

That's how you control your narrative before someone else does. Not by being first with a message. By being consistent with the truth.

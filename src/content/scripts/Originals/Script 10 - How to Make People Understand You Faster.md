# SCRIPT 10: "How to Make People Understand You Faster"

## CHAPTER 1: THE MISUNDERSTANDING PROBLEM

Here's something I keep coming back to. Most people who feel misunderstood aren't misunderstood because they're complicated. They're misunderstood because they're unclear. There's a difference. And the difference matters a lot for what you do about it.

Being complicated means the ideas themselves are complex. They require time, context, and careful explanation to be understood. That's real. Some ideas are genuinely complex.

Being unclear means the communication is creating friction that doesn't need to be there. The ideas might be straightforward, but the way they're being communicated is making them harder to understand than they need to be. That's a different problem. And it's a much more solvable one.

Most people who struggle to be understood are dealing with the second problem, not the first. Their ideas aren't too complex. Their communication is creating unnecessary friction. And that friction is the thing that can be fixed.

I want to investigate how understanding actually works. What the brain does when it's trying to form a model of someone. What creates friction in that process. And what the fastest path to being understood actually is.

## CHAPTER 2: HOW FIRST IMPRESSIONS FORM

Here's the mechanism. The brain forms first impressions extremely quickly. Research suggests it happens in milliseconds for visual impressions, and within the first few seconds for verbal ones. And those first impressions are remarkably durable. They anchor subsequent interpretation. New information gets filtered through them.

This is the anchoring effect. The first piece of information the brain receives about a person becomes the anchor for everything that follows. Subsequent information gets interpreted in light of the anchor. Information that confirms the anchor gets noted. Information that contradicts it gets treated as an exception.

This means that the first impression you create — whether it's the first thing someone reads about you, the first video they watch, the first time they hear you speak — has disproportionate influence on how they understand you. It sets the frame. It establishes the anchor. And everything that comes after gets interpreted through it.

This is why the beginning of any communication is so important. Not just the hook. The entire opening frame. The first impression of who you are and what you're about. Because that impression is going to shape how everything else lands.

## CHAPTER 3: THE SPECIFICITY MECHANISM

Here's what makes first impressions clear or unclear. Specificity.

When the first impression is specific — when it gives the brain concrete, precise information about who you are and what you do — the brain can form an accurate model quickly. It knows what to expect. It knows where to file you. It knows how to interpret what comes next.

When the first impression is vague — when it gives the brain general, abstract information that could apply to many people — the brain can't form an accurate model. It doesn't know what to expect. It doesn't know where to file you. And it defaults to generic assumptions that may have nothing to do with who you actually are.

This is the specificity mechanism. Specific information creates clear models. Vague information creates unclear ones. And unclear models are the root of most misunderstanding.

Here's what specificity looks like in practice. Not "I help businesses grow." But "I help business owners use video to build authority with the people they're trying to reach." Not "I make content." But "I investigate the psychology of why people avoid being visible, and what happens when they do it anyway."

The specific version takes more words. But it creates a much clearer model. The brain knows exactly what to do with it. It knows what to expect. It knows how to interpret what comes next. And it's much less likely to misunderstand.

## CHAPTER 4: THE ABSTRACTION TRAP

Here's where most people go wrong. They communicate in abstractions. They talk about concepts rather than behaviors. They describe ideas rather than showing what they look like. They tell people what to think rather than showing them what to observe.

This is the abstraction trap. And it's particularly common among people who know their subject well. Because when you know something deeply, the abstractions feel concrete to you. The concepts feel specific. The ideas feel tangible. But to someone who doesn't have your background, those same abstractions are genuinely abstract. They don't have the context to make them concrete.

The solution is to translate. To take the abstract concept and show what it looks like in observable behavior. To take the general idea and ground it in a specific example. To take the complex theory and demonstrate it with a concrete case.

This translation is the work of clarity. And it's harder than it sounds. Because it requires you to see your subject from the outside. To imagine what it looks like to someone who doesn't already understand it. To find the specific, concrete, observable version of the abstract idea you're trying to communicate.

But when you do that work — when you translate consistently — the result is communication that creates understanding quickly. That doesn't require the audience to do the translation themselves. That meets them where they are rather than requiring them to come to where you are.

## CHAPTER 5: THE CONSISTENCY REQUIREMENT

Here's the dimension of this that most people miss. Being understood isn't just about individual pieces of communication. It's about the pattern across all of them.

The brain builds its model of you over time. From multiple interactions. Multiple pieces of content. Multiple encounters. And the model it builds is based on the pattern it observes, not just on any single interaction.

This means that clarity in individual communications isn't enough if the pattern across communications is inconsistent. If you're specific about one thing in one piece of content and specific about a different thing in another, the brain doesn't build a clear model. It builds a confused one. It doesn't know which specificity to anchor on. Which version of you to expect.

Consistency of specificity is what builds a clear model over time. The same specific focus. The same specific frame. The same specific perspective, applied consistently across everything you create.

This is why direction matters so much for being understood. Not just for building a presence. But for being understood accurately. Consistent direction creates consistent specificity. And consistent specificity creates a clear, durable model that people can rely on.

## CHAPTER 6: WHAT FAST UNDERSTANDING LOOKS LIKE

Here's the practical version. Making people understand you faster is about reducing the friction between who you are and the model people build of you.

It starts with being specific about what you're about. Not in a marketing way. In a genuine way. The clearest signal of who you are isn't your bio or your tagline. It's the specificity of what you talk about and how you talk about it.

It continues with translating abstractions into observable behaviors. Every time you catch yourself using an abstract concept, ask: what does this look like in practice? What would someone observe if they were watching this happen? What's the specific, concrete version of this idea?

It requires consistency. The same specific focus, applied consistently across everything you create. So that the model people build of you gets stronger and clearer with each interaction rather than more confused.

And it requires honesty about where you are. Not performing a version of yourself that's further along than you actually are. Not claiming expertise you haven't earned. Just showing up as you actually are, doing the work you're actually doing, thinking about the things you're actually thinking about.

That honesty is what makes understanding durable. A performed version of yourself creates a model that eventually collapses when reality contradicts it. An honest version creates a model that gets stronger over time because it's built on something real.

## CHAPTER 7: THE INSIGHT

Here's the uncomfortable truth about being understood. Most people who feel misunderstood are contributing to their own misunderstanding. Not intentionally. But through the vagueness, the abstraction, the inconsistency, and the performance that create the friction they're trying to overcome.

The path to being understood faster isn't to explain yourself more. It's to be clearer about what you're about. More specific. More consistent. More honest.

And here's the paradox. The more specific you are, the smaller your apparent audience. But the more accurately you're understood by the people who are actually your audience. And being accurately understood by the right people is worth more than being vaguely known by everyone.

The people who are most understood are almost never the ones who try to appeal to everyone. They're the ones who are so specific about what they're about that the people who need what they offer can find them immediately. Who recognize them immediately. Who trust them immediately.

That's the goal. Not to be understood by everyone. To be understood accurately by the people who matter. And that requires the discipline of specificity. Of consistency. Of honesty. Of doing the translation work that makes complex ideas accessible without losing their substance.

That's how you make people understand you faster. Not by explaining more. By being clearer about what you're actually about.

Proverbs 4:7 says: "The beginning of wisdom is this: Get wisdom. Though it cost all you have, get understanding." Understanding — being understood and understanding others — is worth the investment. The investment of clarity. Of specificity. Of the discipline to translate what you know into what others can receive.

## EXTENDED: THE TRANSLATION DISCIPLINE

Here's what the translation discipline actually looks like in practice. Because I think it's easy to say "translate abstractions into concrete examples" and hard to actually do it.

The discipline starts with a question you ask about every abstract statement you make: what would someone observe if they were watching this happen? Not what does this mean conceptually. What would they see? What would they hear? What would they notice?

That question forces you out of the abstract and into the observable. And the observable is almost always more communicable than the abstract.

Here's an example. The abstract version: "People adjust their behavior when they're being observed." The observable version: "You adjust your posture without realizing it. You sit up straighter. You stop slouching. You become presentable. Not comfortable. Presentable."

The second version communicates the same idea. But it does it through specific, observable behavior. The audience can see it. They can recognize it. They can feel it. And because they can feel it, they understand it — not just intellectually, but experientially.

That experiential understanding is what makes content land. Not just the intellectual comprehension of an idea. The felt recognition of something you've experienced. The "oh, that's what that is" moment.

That moment is what you're aiming for with every piece of content. Not "that's interesting." But "oh, that's what that is." The recognition of something familiar, named accurately for the first time.

That recognition is the fastest path to being understood. Not explaining more. Naming more accurately. Describing more specifically. Showing what it looks like rather than telling what it means.

## THE AUDIENCE MODELING REQUIREMENT

Here's the final dimension of making people understand you faster. You have to know who you're talking to. Not in a demographic way. In a cognitive way. What do they already know? What context do they have? What vocabulary do they share with you? What do you need to explain, and what can you assume?

This is the audience modeling requirement. And it's where a lot of content fails. Not because the communicator doesn't know their subject. But because they don't know their audience well enough to calibrate the communication correctly.

When you calibrate too high — when you assume more knowledge than your audience has — you create confusion. The audience doesn't have the context to understand what you're saying. They feel lost. They disengage.

When you calibrate too low — when you explain things your audience already knows — you create boredom. The audience feels patronized. They feel like you don't respect their intelligence. They disengage.

The calibration sweet spot is where you're meeting the audience exactly where they are. Explaining what they need explained. Assuming what they already know. Speaking to them as intelligent people who don't yet have your specific knowledge.

That calibration is what makes content feel like it was made for you. Like the person making it actually knows who they're talking to. And that feeling — of being seen, of being understood, of being spoken to directly — is what creates the deepest engagement.

That's the goal. Not to be understood by everyone. To be understood accurately by the people you're trying to reach. And that requires knowing who they are well enough to calibrate your communication to where they actually are.

That's how you make people understand you faster. Not by explaining more. By knowing your audience well enough to explain exactly what they need, in exactly the way they can receive it.

## THE FINAL WORD ON BEING UNDERSTOOD

Here's the thing about being understood that I keep coming back to. It's not a passive process. You don't get understood by hoping people will figure out what you're about. You get understood by doing the work of being clear.

That work is ongoing. It doesn't end when you've written a good bio or made a good video. It continues with every piece of content you create. Every interaction where someone encounters you. Every opportunity to be specific, to be honest, to be consistent.

The people who are most understood are the ones who do that work consistently. Who show up with the same specific perspective, the same honest voice, the same clear direction — over and over, until the model people have of them is accurate and strong.

That's the goal. Not to be understood once. To be understood consistently. By the people who matter. In a way that's accurate enough to be useful.

And that requires the ongoing discipline of clarity. Of specificity. Of consistency. Of doing the translation work that makes your ideas accessible without losing their substance.

That's how you make people understand you faster. Not by explaining more. By being clearer about what you're actually about. Consistently. Over time. Until the model people have of you is the one you actually want them to have.

## THE UNDERSTANDING FEEDBACK LOOP

Here's something practical about making people understand you faster. You can't fully know whether you're being understood without feedback. And feedback requires showing up — creating content, sharing it, observing how people respond.

The response tells you things you can't know in advance. Which parts land. Which parts confuse. Which examples resonate. Which abstractions need more translation. Which vocabulary is shared and which needs to be explained.

That information is only available through doing. Through creating content and observing how it's received. Through the feedback loop that only runs when you're actually showing up.

This is why the fastest path to being understood isn't to plan the perfect communication strategy. It's to start communicating and learn from the response. To treat each piece of content as an experiment. To observe what lands and what doesn't. To adjust based on what you learn. And to do it again.

The understanding improves through iteration. Not through planning. Through doing, learning, adjusting, doing again.

And here's the thing about that iteration. It's not just that your communication gets better. Your understanding of your audience gets better. You learn more about who they are, what they already know, what they need explained, what vocabulary they share with you. And that knowledge makes every subsequent piece of content more effective.

The feedback loop is the engine of improvement. And it only runs when you're actually showing up and creating. Not when you're planning to.

That's the final word on making people understand you faster. Start the feedback loop. Create. Observe. Adjust. Create again. And let the understanding improve through the process of doing.

Because the fastest path to being understood isn't the perfect communication. It's the consistent practice of communicating, learning, and improving. Over time. Until being understood is the default.
